What Is This Assignment?

You have a budget (Week 7). Now the harder question: where does the money actually come from, and how do you get it?

You submit your strategy document to Webcourses. Your fundraising strategy feeds directly into the Strategic Plan (Week 9, 10 pts), so build it with that level of seriousness.

In PAD 5146, you produced a comprehensive Fundraising Plan — including a fundraising audit, SWOT analysis, donor cultivation strategies, and an ethics and stewardship narrative. That was for an established organization. Now you're designing a fundraising strategy for your own startup, where you have no donor history, no track record, and no brand recognition. The challenge is fundamentally different.

What Do I Submit?

1. Fundraising SWOT Analysis

Before designing your strategy, assess your starting position honestly.

HelpfulHarmful
InternalStrengths — What assets do you have? (passionate founder, clear mission, board connections, community trust)Weaknesses — What's missing? (no donor database, no brand recognition, no fundraising staff, limited network)
ExternalOpportunities — What's in your favor? (growing community need, foundations prioritizing your issue, local corporate interest)Threats — What could block you? (competition for grants, economic downturn, donor fatigue, political climate)

2. Revenue Strategy by Source

For each revenue source in your Week 7 budget, develop a specific plan for how you'll actually secure that funding. Don't just list amounts — describe the who, what, and how.

Foundation Grants

  • Identify 3–5 specific foundations that fund organizations like yours. Use Candid's Foundation Directory (free through UCF Library databases) and Candid's free 990 search (candid.org) to find foundations that have funded similar organizations in your area.
  • For each foundation, note: name, typical grant size, focus area, and application timeline
  • Which grants will you apply for in Year 1? Be realistic — most foundations have 6–12 month cycles

Individual Donations

  • Who are your first donors? (Board members, personal networks, community leaders)
  • What is your give/get policy for board members?
  • How will you build a donor pipeline from zero? Describe your donor cultivation strategy using Perplexity.ai to research best practices for startup nonprofit fundraising

Corporate Sponsorships

  • Identify 3–5 local businesses that might sponsor your organization
  • What do you offer in return? (Logo on website, event naming, social media mentions, community goodwill)
  • Research corporate giving programs using Perplexity.ai: "What corporations in the Orlando area have community giving programs that support immigrant services or social services?"

Events and Community Fundraising

  • Plan one fundraising event for Year 1
  • Estimated gross revenue vs. net revenue (events are expensive — be honest about costs)
  • Use Canva to design a mock event flyer or save-the-date graphic

Government Grants and Contracts

  • Are there local, state, or federal funding opportunities for your services?
  • Search Grants.gov (grants.gov) for federal opportunities and your county/city government website for local RFPs
  • Be honest: most government funding requires an established track record. What's your path to eligibility?

3. Donor Communications Plan

How will you communicate with donors and prospects?

  • Email newsletter — What platform will you use? (Mailchimp offers a free tier for up to 500 contacts.) How often will you send it? What content?
  • Social media — Which platforms? What's your posting strategy?
  • Annual report — Even in Year 1, plan for a simple impact report to send to all donors and prospects
  • Thank-you process — How quickly will you acknowledge gifts? Through what channels?
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Donor Communications
  • Email: Monthly newsletter via Mailchimp (free tier) — community impact stories, upcoming events, volunteer spotlights
  • Social media: Instagram (primary — visual storytelling) + Facebook (community groups, event promotion) + LinkedIn (professional network, corporate prospects). Use Canva to create templated social media graphics that maintain brand consistency
  • Annual report: Simple 4-page PDF designed in Canva — distributed by email and posted on website by December Year 1
  • Thank-you: Personalized email within 48 hours of any gift; handwritten note from ED for gifts over $100; board chair call for gifts over $500

4. Ethics and Stewardship

Draw on the ethics and stewardship narrative from your PAD 5146 plan:

  • What are your organization's ethical commitments around fundraising?
  • How will you ensure donor intent is honored?
  • What gifts would you decline, and why?
  • How will you maintain transparency with donors about how funds are used?
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Ethics Statement

Asian Bridge Orlando commits to ethical fundraising aligned with the Association of Fundraising Professionals (AFP) Code of Ethics. Specifically:

  • We will not accept gifts from organizations whose activities conflict with our mission or the interests of the communities we serve
  • All donor-restricted gifts will be used exclusively for the stated purpose
  • We will publish annual financial summaries on our website's Transparency page
  • We will not share donor information with third parties without explicit consent

This ethics framework was informed by my PAD 5146 analysis, where I found that the organization I studied lacked a clear gift acceptance policy — leading to a situation where they accepted a corporate sponsorship that created a perceived conflict of interest with their advocacy work.

Website Update

Add a "Donate" or "Support Us" page to your website. This page should make it easy for someone who's ready to give — and compelling for someone who's still deciding.

Your page should include:

  • A clear statement of why donations matter — connect giving to impact, not to your budget gap
  • Giving levels with descriptions of what each level supports (e.g., "$50 covers translation services for one family intake session")
  • A call to action — even without a real payment processor, include a "Donate" button or a "Contact us to give" prompt
  • Information about other ways to support: volunteering, corporate sponsorships, in-kind donations
  • Use Canva to design a visual giving-level graphic or impact infographic for the page
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Donate Page

Page title: Support Our Work

Intro: "Every dollar you give helps an immigrant family navigate a system that wasn't designed with them in mind. Your support funds translation services, community navigation, legal clinic referrals, and the infrastructure that makes it all possible."

Giving Levels:

  • $25 — Welcome Kit: Covers a multilingual resource packet for one newly arrived family
  • $50 — First Steps: Funds one family intake and navigation session with a bilingual community navigator
  • $150 — Bridge Builder: Supports one family through a full month of community navigation services
  • $500 — Community Champion: Sponsors a community workshop (healthcare enrollment, school registration, or civic participation)
  • $1,000 — Founding Circle: Become a founding donor — your name on our annual report and an invitation to our year-end celebration

Other Ways to Give: Corporate sponsorships · In-kind donations · Volunteer your time · Include us in your will

What Is This Assignment?

In PAD 6237, you conducted a Governance Case Study — you analyzed a real nonprofit's board structure, commended good practices, and made recommendations for improvement. That was about evaluating someone else's governance.

Now you're on the other side. You're designing governance from scratch for the nonprofit you're building. The question isn't "what's wrong with their board?" — it's "what should my board look like, and why?"

This assignment asks you to take what you learned in PAD 6237 and apply it to your own organization. You'll produce a formal Board Governance Policy Document, and then publish the key elements as a page on your website.

The Process: From Your PAD 6237 Work to Your New Organization

This is the core workflow for all Capstone Project deliverables — you'll repeat this pattern throughout the semester:

Step 1: Revisit Your Prior Coursework

Pull up your PAD 6237 Governance Case Study. Reread your analysis, your recommendations, and the governance principles you identified as best practices.

Step 2: Upload to Google NotebookLM and Discuss

This is where AI becomes a genuine thinking partner — not a content generator.

Using Google NotebookLM for This Assignment

NotebookLM (notebooklm.google.com) is different from ChatGPT or Claude. Instead of generating text from general knowledge, it works from your own documents. This makes it ideal for adapting your prior coursework.

How to use it:

  1. Go to notebooklm.google.com (free with Google account)
  2. Create a new notebook
  3. Upload your PAD 6237 Governance Case Study (PDF or Google Doc)
  4. Optionally upload: the BoardSource Handbook chapter on governance, Worth Ch. 4, or other relevant readings
  5. Start a conversation with your sources:
Based on my governance case study, what were the strongest governance
practices I identified? How would I adapt those for a brand-new
nonprofit serving Asian American and immigrant communities in Central
Florida with a 9-member board?
In my case study, I recommended improvements to the organization's
conflict of interest policy. Help me draft a conflict of interest
policy for my new organization that incorporates those recommendations
from the start.
What governance challenges might be unique to an organization where
board members are also members of the community being served?
How should the bylaws address this?

The key advantage: NotebookLM's responses are grounded in your actual analysis, not generic governance templates. It helps you build on your own intellectual work.

Step 3: Draft Your Governance Policy

Using insights from your NotebookLM discussion, draft the Board Governance Policy for your organization (see sections below).

Step 4: Update Your Website

Publish a summary of your governance structure as a "Board" or "Leadership" page on your nonprofit website.

What Do I Submit?

A 5–7 page, double-spaced Board Governance Policy Document covering:

1. Board Mission and Role

  • What is the board's purpose within your organization?
  • How does the board uphold public service values and the organization's mission?
  • Reference: How does this reflect what you learned about effective governance in PAD 6237?

2. Board Composition Policy

  • Board size (typically 7–15 for a new nonprofit)
  • Term lengths and term limits
  • Diversity expectations — how will you ensure the board reflects the community you serve?
  • Recruitment strategy — where will you find board members?
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Board Composition

Board size: 9 members (expandable to 13)
Term: 3 years, renewable once (6-year maximum)

Composition priorities:

  • At least 3 members from the Asian American and immigrant community being served — including representation from East Asian, South Asian, and Southeast Asian backgrounds
  • At least 1 member with legal expertise (immigration law preferred)
  • At least 1 member with nonprofit financial management experience
  • At least 1 member with fundraising/development background
  • At least 1 member under 30 to ensure intergenerational perspective
  • Board must reflect the linguistic diversity of the community served — at minimum, Mandarin, Vietnamese, and Burmese/Karen language capacity across the full board

Recruitment strategy: Nominating committee will identify candidates through community partnerships, UCF alumni networks, local bar associations (for legal expertise), and direct outreach to community leaders.

Note: This composition plan was informed by my PAD 6237 case study, where I found that the organization I analyzed lacked meaningful representation from the community it served. That governance gap reduced trust and limited the board's ability to make culturally informed decisions. Asian Bridge Orlando's composition policy is designed to prevent that problem from the start.

3. Committee Structure

  • Standing committees (e.g., Executive, Finance, Fundraising/Development, Program, Governance/Nominating)
  • Each committee's purpose and membership expectations

4. Board Member Responsibilities

  • Fiduciary duties (duty of care, duty of loyalty, duty of obedience)
  • Give/get policy — financial contribution expectations
  • Attendance and participation expectations
  • Ambassadorship — representing the organization in the community

5. Conflict of Interest Policy

  • A full written policy (1–2 pages within the document)
  • Disclosure requirements
  • Procedures for handling conflicts
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Conflict of Interest Excerpt

Section 3: Disclosure Requirements

Each board member shall annually sign a Conflict of Interest Disclosure Statement identifying any relationships, positions, or circumstances that could contribute to a conflict of interest, including but not limited to: (a) employment or consulting relationships with organizations that do business with Asian Bridge Orlando; (b) family relationships with staff, vendors, or major donors; (c) financial interests in entities that contract with the organization.

Section 4: Community Member Board Service

Because Asian Bridge Orlando's board intentionally includes members of the community being served, the organization acknowledges that some board members may also be recipients of organizational services. This dual relationship is permitted and encouraged as essential to mission-aligned governance. However, board members in this situation must recuse themselves from any vote directly affecting services they personally receive, and must disclose the relationship annually.

6. Meeting Structure and Quorum Rules

  • Meeting frequency (quarterly is standard; monthly for startups)
  • Quorum requirements
  • Procedures for remote participation and voting

For Florida-specific governance requirements — including board composition rules, filing obligations, and annual report deadlines — check the National Council of Nonprofits (councilofnonprofits.org) state-by-state resource page and the Florida Division of Corporations at Sunbiz.org. Reviewing actual articles of incorporation filed by similar Florida nonprofits on Sunbiz can help you understand the legal language and structure your governance document should reflect.

Website Update

Real nonprofit websites don't post their full governance policy documents online. What they do post: a clear, concise Leadership page that tells visitors who governs the organization and how. Your website should work the same way.

Add a "Board" or "Leadership" page with:

  • A brief governance philosophy statement (2–3 sentences)
  • Board member names and bios (fictional for now — but write them as if they're real). Use Canva AI to create a clean board member profile grid with photo placeholders and bios
  • Committee structure (a simple list or table)
  • A "Download" link to the full Board Governance Policy (upload as PDF or link to a Google Doc)
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Board Page Structure

Page title: Our Leadership

Intro paragraph:
"Asian Bridge Orlando is governed by a volunteer Board of Directors committed to representing the communities we serve. Our board includes professionals from the legal, nonprofit, and healthcare sectors alongside community leaders from Central Florida's diverse Asian American and immigrant populations."

Board Members: (listed with photo placeholder, name, title, 1-sentence bio)

  • Jane Nguyen, Board Chair — Immigration attorney, 15 years of community advocacy
  • David Park, Treasurer — CPA with nonprofit financial management experience
  • Rina Gurung, Secretary — Bhutanese community organizer and former refugee
  • (etc.)

Committees: Executive · Finance · Programs · Governance & Nominating · Fundraising

Footer:
Download our full Board Governance Policy (PDF)

How to do this efficiently:

  1. Write the full policy document in Word (your graded submission)
  2. Save as PDF
  3. Upload the PDF to your website (Google Sites: Insert → File; Wix: Add → Document)
  4. Write the summary content directly on the web page (~15 minutes)
  5. Link the PDF with a download button

This is one deliverable in two formats — the academic version (Word, submitted to Webcourses) and the professional version (web summary + PDF download, on your site). This is exactly how you'd do it if this organization were real.

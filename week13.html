What Is This Assignment?

Nonprofits don't just deliver services — they advocate for the people they serve. The communities your organization works with face systemic barriers that no single program can fix. Policy change is how you address root causes, not just symptoms.

In PAD 6142, you developed a Policy Advocacy Plan for a nonprofit organization — identifying a policy issue, mapping the political landscape, and designing an advocacy strategy within 501(c)(3) legal boundaries. Now you're doing the same for your own organization: what policy issue most affects your community, and what can your nonprofit legally and strategically do about it?

This assignment is also a test of a critical nonprofit leadership skill: understanding what you can and cannot do as a tax-exempt organization. 501(c)(3)s can educate, research, and advocate — but they cannot endorse candidates or spend a "substantial part" of their activities on lobbying. Knowing where the line is matters.

What Do I Submit?

1. Policy Issue Identification (1 page)

Identify one specific policy issue that directly affects your organization's community. Be precise — not "immigration reform" but rather "Florida's lack of mandated language access standards in county health departments."

For each policy issue, address: - What is the current policy (or lack of policy)? - Who is affected, and how? - What level of government has jurisdiction? (local, state, federal) - What is the current political landscape around this issue?

Use Perplexity.ai to research the current status: "What is Florida's current policy on language access requirements in healthcare settings? What legislation has been proposed in 2024–2026?"

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Policy Issue

Issue: Lack of mandated language access standards in Orange County healthcare facilities.

Current situation: While federal law (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act) requires recipients of federal funds to provide meaningful language access, there is no Florida state law or Orange County ordinance establishing specific standards for translation and interpretation in healthcare settings. Compliance is inconsistent — our Week 11 case study cited an Orlando Sentinel investigation documenting translation failures at three local hospitals.

Who is affected: An estimated 85,000 residents in Orange County speak English "less than very well" (Census Reporter, 2024). For Asian American and immigrant families, medical miscommunication creates risks ranging from incorrect prescriptions to missed diagnoses.

Jurisdiction: County level — Orange County Board of County Commissioners has authority to establish local ordinances and to set standards for county-funded health programs.

Political landscape: Orange County has a history of progressive policy on immigrant inclusion (e.g., community ID program). Two current commissioners have publicly supported language access initiatives. The local medical association has expressed openness to voluntary standards but opposes unfunded mandates.

2. Advocacy Goals and Objectives (half a page)

Define what your organization is trying to achieve through advocacy. Use SMART criteria: - Goal: The broad policy change you seek - Objectives: Specific, measurable steps toward that goal

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Advocacy Goals

Goal: Establish minimum language access standards for healthcare facilities in Orange County that receive county funding.

Objectives: 1. By March 2027, present a community impact report to the Orange County Board of County Commissioners documenting language access gaps in local healthcare 2. By June 2027, secure commitments from at least 3 county commissioners to support a language access ordinance 3. By December 2027, contribute to the drafting and introduction of a county language access ordinance

3. 501(c)(3) Legal Framework (half a page)

Demonstrate that you understand what your organization can and cannot do:

Activity Permitted? Notes
Voter education (nonpartisan) ✅ Yes Candidate forums, voter guides (must be nonpartisan)
Issue advocacy ✅ Yes Educating the public and policymakers about your issue — no limits
Direct lobbying ⚠️ Limited Contacting legislators about specific legislation — permitted but cannot be a "substantial part" of activities
Grassroots lobbying ⚠️ Limited Asking the public to contact legislators about specific bills — same limits apply
Candidate endorsement ❌ No Absolutely prohibited for 501(c)(3)s
Campaign contributions ❌ No Absolutely prohibited

Use ChatGPT or Claude to help clarify: "Explain the IRS rules on 501(c)(3) lobbying limits. What is the 'substantial part' test vs. the 501(h) election? Which is better for a small nonprofit?"

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Legal Framework

Asian Bridge Orlando will operate under the 501(h) election, which provides clear expenditure limits for lobbying rather than the vague "substantial part" test. Under 501(h), organizations with budgets under $500,000 may spend up to 20% of exempt purpose expenditures on lobbying. For our $200,000 budget, this allows up to $40,000 in lobbying-related activities — well above what we plan to spend.

The majority of our advocacy work — community education, research, coalition building — qualifies as issue advocacy, which has no spending limits under IRS rules.

4. Advocacy Strategy (2–3 pages)

Design your advocacy approach using multiple tactics. For each tactic, explain what you'll do, who is responsible, and what resources are needed.

Community Education and Research

  • How will you educate your community about the policy issue?
  • What data or research will you produce to support your position?
  • Use Google Scholar to find academic research supporting your policy position

Coalition Building

  • What organizations will you partner with on this issue?
  • How will a coalition amplify your impact beyond what your organization could do alone?
  • Use Candid / GuideStar (candid.org) to identify potential coalition partners — search for organizations working on similar issues in your area

Policymaker Engagement

  • Who are the specific policymakers you need to reach? (Name them)
  • How will you build relationships? (meetings, testimony, policy briefs, invitations to see your programs)
  • What is your "ask" — what specific action do you want each policymaker to take?

Public Awareness

  • How will you bring public attention to the issue?
  • What communications channels will you use? (social media, op-eds, community events, media outreach)
  • Use Canva to design an advocacy one-pager or infographic that summarizes the issue for a general audience

Community Mobilization

  • How will you engage the people most affected by this policy?
  • How will you make it easy for community members to take action? (petitions, public comment, attending hearings)

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Advocacy Strategy (Excerpt)

Policymaker Engagement: - Schedule meetings with Commissioners [Name] and [Name] — both have expressed support for immigrant inclusion initiatives - Present community impact data at a County Commission public comment session - Invite commissioners to observe a Community Navigator session firsthand — let them see the language barrier in practice - Deliver a one-page policy brief (designed in Canva) summarizing the issue, the data, and the proposed solution

Community Mobilization: - Partner with AARC, Catholic Charities, and UCF's Office of Diversity to form a Language Access Coalition - Host a community forum (bilingual, with interpretation) where affected families share their experiences - Create a simple online petition via Google Forms — collect signatures supporting the language access ordinance - Train 10 community members to deliver 2-minute testimonials at County Commission hearings

Website Update: "Advocacy / Take Action" Page

Add an "Advocacy" or "Take Action" page to your website. This page should make visitors understand the policy issue and give them something concrete to do.

Your page should include:

1. "The Issue" — 2–3 sentences explaining the policy problem in plain language (not academic language — a community member should understand it)

2. "What We're Asking For" — your specific policy ask, clearly stated

3. "How You Can Help" — 2–3 concrete actions a visitor can take right now (sign a petition, contact a commissioner, attend a hearing, share on social media)

4. "Who to Contact" — names and contact information for the relevant policymakers

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Take Action Page

Page title: Take Action

The Issue: "Thousands of families in Orange County struggle to communicate with their doctors, navigate the school system, or access public services — simply because services aren't available in their language. There is currently no county standard requiring language access in publicly funded healthcare."

What We're Asking For: "We're calling on the Orange County Board of County Commissioners to pass a Language Access Ordinance — establishing minimum translation and interpretation standards for all county-funded healthcare facilities."

How You Can Help: - 📝 Sign our community petition [link to Google Form] - 📧 Email your County Commissioner — [sample email template] - 📅 Attend the next County Commission meeting — [date, time, location] - 📢 Share this page with your network

Who to Contact: - Commissioner [Name], District [X] — email, phone - Commissioner [Name], District [Y] — email, phone - Orange County Mayor [Name] — email, phone

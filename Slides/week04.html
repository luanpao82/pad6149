Worth Ch. 4

Why Governance Matters

The board is the legal and ethical backbone of every nonprofit organization.

Fiduciary Duty

Ensuring the organization's resources are used properly and in furtherance of its mission. Financial oversight, audit responsibility, asset protection.

Duty of Care

Exercising reasonable care in decision-making. Attending meetings, reviewing materials, asking informed questions, staying engaged.

Duty of Loyalty

Putting the organization's interests above personal interests. Disclosing conflicts, avoiding self-dealing, acting in good faith.

Board members are stewards, not owners. They hold the organization in trust for the community it serves.

Worth Chapter 4 lays the foundation. A nonprofit's board of directors is not advisory — it is the legal governing body. Board members bear three fundamental duties: fiduciary duty (protecting assets and managing money responsibly), duty of care (making informed decisions, actually reading the financials before meetings), and duty of loyalty (no conflicts of interest, no self-dealing). When boards fail at these, you get scandals — think of the United Way controversy in the 1990s or more recent cases. For your nonprofit, you need to design a board that takes these duties seriously from day one.
Miller-Millesen (2003)

Board Roles & Responsibilities

Three theoretical lenses explain why boards behave the way they do:

Theory Board Role Key Focus
Agency Theory Monitoring & oversight Ensure management acts in the organization's interest; reduce information asymmetry between staff and board
Resource Dependency Resource acquisition Board members bring money, connections, expertise, and legitimacy; link the organization to its environment
Institutional Theory Conformity & legitimacy Boards adopt structures and practices that signal legitimacy to funders, regulators, and the public

Implication for your nonprofit: When you design your board, think about which role you need most right now. A startup board is usually heavy on resource dependency — you need people who can open doors.

Miller-Millesen's 2003 article is one of the most-cited pieces on board behavior. She synthesizes three major theories. Agency theory says boards exist to monitor management — think of it as a principal-agent problem. Resource dependency theory says boards exist to bring in resources: money, expertise, political connections, community legitimacy. Institutional theory says boards adopt certain structures because that's what funders and regulators expect — you have a board because you're supposed to have a board. In reality, all three operate simultaneously. For your startup nonprofit, resource dependency is probably the most critical lens. You need board members who can actually help you get things done, not just rubber-stamp decisions.
Abzug & Galaskiewicz (2001)

Board Composition

Expertise vs. Local Identity

  • Expertise model: Recruit for skills — legal, finance, fundraising, marketing, program area knowledge
  • Identity model: Recruit for representation — community members, constituents, demographic diversity
  • Effective boards balance both — skills the organization needs AND voices from communities it serves

Recruitment Strategies

  • Skills matrix: map current gaps
  • Term limits: ensure rotation
  • Pipeline: advisory boards, committees
  • Intentional diversity: race, gender, age, sector, lived experience

For Your Workbook

Draft a board composition plan. Consider:

  • Board size (5–15 for a startup)
  • Desired expertise areas
  • Community representation goals
  • Term length and limits
  • Officer positions (Chair, Vice-Chair, Secretary, Treasurer)
  • Standing committees (Finance, Governance, Programs, Fundraising)
Abzug and Galaskiewicz's research shows a tension in board recruitment: do you prioritize expertise or community identity? The answer is both, but how you balance them depends on your organization's stage and mission. A startup might lean toward expertise — you need someone who understands nonprofit accounting, someone with legal knowledge, someone with fundraising experience. But if you're serving a specific community, having board members from that community is not optional — it's an equity and accountability issue. In your workbook this week, you should be drafting your ideal board composition. Use a skills matrix approach: list the competencies you need, then identify the gaps. Most small nonprofits start with 5 to 9 board members. You can grow from there.
Hall & Kennedy (2008)

The “New Governance”

Public and nonprofit governance is evolving beyond the traditional hierarchical board model.

Collaborative Governance

Decision-making shared across organizations, sectors, and stakeholders. Boards as conveners, not just directors.

Network Governance

Nonprofits operate in networks — funders, government agencies, peer organizations. Board members serve as boundary spanners.

Adaptive Governance

Governance structures must evolve as the organization grows. What works for a startup board won't work at scale.

Key takeaway: Your board structure should be designed for where your nonprofit is now, with a plan for how it will evolve. Build in flexibility through bylaws and governance policies.

Hall and Kennedy argue that the old model — a board that meets quarterly, reviews financials, and rubber-stamps the ED's decisions — is dying. The new governance is collaborative, networked, and adaptive. Your board members need to be connectors who bring your organization into larger conversations. They need to collaborate with other organizations, government partners, and community stakeholders. And your governance structure needs to evolve. The bylaws you write for a three-person founding board should have provisions for growth — adding seats, creating committees, changing term structures. This is directly relevant to your governance policy this week.
Stone & Ostrower (2007) NASPAA C4

Accountability & Public Interest

Boards Acting in the Public Interest

  • Nonprofits receive public trust through tax-exempt status — boards must honor that trust
  • Accountability is multi-directional: to donors, to constituents, to regulators, to the public
  • Transparency mechanisms: Form 990, annual reports, open meetings, conflict of interest policies

Democratic Governance

  • Civil society depends on nonprofits acting as democratic institutions
  • Community voice, constituent representation, participatory decision-making

NASPAA Competency 4: Articulate and apply a public service perspective. Your board governance policy should reflect your nonprofit's commitment to democratic governance, transparency, and accountability to the communities you serve.

Connect to prior coursework: Your PAD 6237 Governance Case Study examined these tensions in a real organization. Now you're designing governance from scratch — apply what you learned.

Stone and Ostrower push us to think about boards not just as management structures but as accountability mechanisms. Nonprofits exist because the public grants them tax-exempt status. In exchange, the public expects transparency and accountability. Your board is the primary vehicle for that accountability. This connects directly to NASPAA Competency 4 — the public service perspective. Your governance policy should explicitly address how your board will maintain transparency, avoid conflicts of interest, represent community interests, and hold itself accountable. Don't just write boilerplate — think about what democratic governance actually means for your specific nonprofit and the community you're trying to serve.

C4 · 10 Points

Board Governance Policy

5–7 pages. Your founding governance document for the board of directors.

Required Sections

  • Board mission and purpose
  • Board composition (size, skills, diversity goals)
  • Committee structure and responsibilities
  • Board member roles and duties
  • Conflict of interest policy
  • Meeting rules and decision-making process
  • Term length and succession plan

Reflection Component

  • How does your board structure reflect democratic governance? (NASPAA C4)
  • What did you learn from your PAD 6237 governance case that you're applying here?
  • How will your board ensure accountability to the community?

Tip: This is not a generic template. Your policy should be tailored to your specific nonprofit — its mission, size, stage, and community context.

The Board Governance Policy is 5 to 7 pages and is essentially your founding governance document. This is what you would hand to a new board member on their first day. It needs to cover: why the board exists and what its mission is, who should be on the board, what committees you'll have, what each member's responsibilities are, your conflict of interest policy, how meetings work, and term lengths. The reflection component is important — connect this to NASPAA Competency 4 and your PAD 6237 case study. Don't just list rules; explain why you designed governance this way for your specific organization.
AI Demo

Draft a Conflict of Interest Policy

Use Claude to generate a first draft, then customize it for your organization.

Prompt:

"I am starting a nonprofit called [Your Org Name] that focuses on [mission area]. Draft a board conflict of interest policy that includes: (1) definition of conflict of interest, (2) disclosure requirements, (3) procedures for managing conflicts, (4) annual disclosure form requirement, and (5) consequences for violations. The policy should be appropriate for a small, newly formed 501(c)(3)."

Remember: AI gives you a starting point. You must customize the output for your specific organization, review it for accuracy, and ensure it aligns with your state's nonprofit laws. Never submit AI-generated text without revision and your own professional judgment.

Here's a practical AI demonstration. A conflict of interest policy is one of the most important governance documents, and it's also one where a template is genuinely helpful as a starting point. Show students the Claude prompt on screen. The key elements are: define what counts as a conflict, require annual disclosure, establish procedures for when conflicts arise, and specify consequences. After getting the AI output, you need to customize it — add your organization's name, adjust the language to fit your context, check it against Florida nonprofit law requirements. This is a great example of AI as a drafting tool, not a final product.
Also Due · 10 Points

Mentor Interview Report

4–5 pages. Reflect on your conversation with a real nonprofit leader.

Required Components

1
Organization Profile: Overview of the mentor's organization — mission, size, programs, funding model, governance structure.
2
Interview Summary: Key questions and responses. What did you learn about their leadership, challenges, and governance experience?
3
3–5 Key Takeaways: The most important insights for your own nonprofit project.
4
Reflection: How will this interview shape your approach to building your nonprofit?

Tips for a Strong Report

  • Prepare 8–10 questions in advance, focused on governance, leadership, and operations
  • Ask follow-up questions — don't just read from a script
  • Take notes during or immediately after the interview
  • Connect takeaways to course readings (especially this week's governance material)
  • Be specific — "I learned that small boards are more agile" is weak; describe the example they gave
The Mentor Interview Report is your other 10-point deliverable this week. By now you should have conducted your interview with a nonprofit leader. The report is 4 to 5 pages and has four components: an organization profile, an interview summary, 3 to 5 key takeaways, and a reflection on how this shapes your own nonprofit project. The strongest reports connect the interview insights back to the course material — if your mentor talked about board challenges, connect it to Miller-Millesen or Stone and Ostrower. Be specific and detailed. Generic summaries earn generic grades.

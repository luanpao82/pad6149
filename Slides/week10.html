Foundations

Performance Management in Nonprofits

Why is performance management harder in the nonprofit sector?

  • Multiple bottom lines: Financial health, mission impact, constituent satisfaction, community benefit — no single metric captures success
  • Diverse stakeholders: Funders want accountability; boards want oversight; staff want autonomy; constituents want results
  • Mission vs. metrics tension: Not everything that matters can be measured, and not everything measured matters
  • Attribution problems: Social change is long-term, complex, and involves many actors — isolating your organization's contribution is difficult

Worth Ch. 6: Managing for performance requires balancing accountability demands with organizational learning. The goal is not just to prove impact — it's to improve it.

Worth's Chapter 6 lays the foundation here. In the private sector, performance management is relatively straightforward — revenue, profit, market share. In nonprofits, we face what scholars call the "multiple bottom lines" problem. Your organization might be financially healthy but failing its mission. It might have outstanding programs but terrible governance. Performance management in this sector requires thinking holistically. The tension between mission and metrics is especially important: funders increasingly demand quantifiable outcomes, but the most meaningful changes — shifts in community wellbeing, empowerment, systemic change — are often the hardest to measure. Hudson's Chapter 2 reinforces this by showing how organizational effectiveness depends on aligning internal capacity with external expectations.
Research

Accountability and Performance

Benjamin (2008) — Administration & Society

Nonprofits are bearing more risk for results than ever before.

  • Performance-based contracts: Government and foundation funding increasingly tied to measurable outcomes
  • Accountability shapes measurement: Organizations measure what funders require, not necessarily what matters most for mission
  • Upward vs. downward accountability: Most accountability flows up (to funders and boards) — rarely down (to constituents and communities)
  • Compliance vs. learning: When accountability is punitive, organizations focus on looking good rather than getting better

Key takeaway: Design your evaluation framework to serve your organization's learning needs — not just funder reporting requirements.

Benjamin's 2008 article is essential reading for understanding the accountability landscape nonprofits operate in today. The shift toward performance-based contracting means nonprofits bear more risk — if you don't hit your targets, you lose funding. This creates perverse incentives: organizations cherry-pick easier-to-serve populations, focus on short-term outputs over long-term outcomes, and measure what's convenient rather than what's meaningful. Benjamin draws the critical distinction between upward accountability — answering to funders and government — and downward accountability — answering to the communities you serve. Most nonprofits are heavy on the former and weak on the latter. As you design your evaluation framework, think about who you are accountable to and whether your metrics actually capture what matters to your constituents.
Research

Productivity Enhancement Strategies

Berman & West (1998)

What strategies do public and nonprofit organizations actually use to improve productivity?

Management-Driven Strategies

  • Total Quality Management (TQM)
  • Benchmarking against peers
  • Performance measurement systems
  • Process reengineering

People-Driven Strategies

  • Employee involvement and empowerment
  • Training and professional development
  • Team-based structures
  • Incentive and recognition programs

Finding: The most effective organizations use both management and people strategies together. Systems alone don't work without culture change.

Berman and West's 1998 study surveyed public and nonprofit organizations about their productivity enhancement strategies. They found two broad categories: management-driven approaches like TQM, benchmarking, and formal performance measurement systems; and people-driven approaches like employee empowerment, training, and team-based work. The key finding is that neither category alone is sufficient. Organizations that implemented management systems without investing in their people saw limited gains. And organizations that focused only on people strategies without systematic measurement couldn't sustain improvements. For your nonprofit, this means your evaluation framework needs to be embedded in an organizational culture that values learning and improvement — not just compliance. Think about how you'll actually use the data you collect to make decisions.

ComponentExample: Youth Mentoring Program

You all built logic models in PAD 6327 — this is review but it's critical review because the logic model is the backbone of your evaluation framework assignment. The logic model maps the theory of change behind your program. Inputs are the resources you invest. Activities are what you do with those resources. Outputs are the direct products — countable things. Outcomes are the changes that result from your outputs. And impact is the long-term, population-level change you're ultimately trying to achieve. The most common mistake I see is confusing outputs with outcomes. Serving 150 youth is an output. Improved school attendance is an outcome. Your logic model needs to clearly articulate the causal chain — why do you believe your activities will lead to your intended outcomes?
Evaluation Design

Evaluation Questions

What do you want to know? What decisions will this information inform?

TypeAsksExample
Process EvaluationAre we doing what we said we'd do?Are mentoring sessions being delivered as planned? Are mentors following the curriculum?
Outcome EvaluationAre participants changing as expected?Did participants' school attendance improve? Did self-efficacy scores increase?
Impact EvaluationDid we cause the change?Is the improvement in attendance attributable to our program, or would it have happened anyway?

For your assignment: Focus on process and outcome evaluation questions. Impact evaluation requires experimental or quasi-experimental designs that are beyond a startup nonprofit's immediate capacity.

Evaluation questions drive everything. Before you decide what data to collect, you need to know what questions you're trying to answer. Process evaluation asks whether you're implementing your program as designed — fidelity questions. Outcome evaluation asks whether participants are changing in the ways your logic model predicts. Impact evaluation asks the hardest question: did YOUR program cause the change, or would it have happened anyway? For your assignment, I want you to focus on process and outcome evaluation. Impact evaluation requires control groups, counterfactuals, and sophisticated research designs — that's important work, but it's not realistic for a startup nonprofit in its first year. What IS realistic is setting up systems to track whether you're delivering your program as planned and whether you're seeing the short-term and intermediate outcomes your logic model predicts.

This is your second competency assignment, worth 10 points, and it maps to NASPAA Competency 2: the ability to analyze, synthesize, think critically, and solve problems. You developed program evaluation skills in PAD 6327 — now you apply them to the nonprofit you've been building all semester. Six sections, 5 to 7 pages. The program description should be concise — one to two paragraphs that clearly describe what your program does, who it serves, and why you believe it will work. The logic model should be visualized — don't just describe it in narrative, show it in a diagram or table. Your evaluation questions should be specific and answerable. The data collection plan needs to be realistic for a startup — you won't have a research department, so think about what a small team can actually collect. Performance indicators need measurable targets — not "improve attendance" but "80 percent of participants attend at least 75 percent of sessions." And the reporting plan should address multiple audiences: your board, your funders, and your community.
AI Demo

Build a Logic Model

Use AI to draft logic model components for your specific program — then refine with your expertise.

ChatGPT / Claude PromptI'm developing a nonprofit program that [brief description of your program, target population, and intended outcomes]. Help me draft a logic model with the following components: - Inputs (resources needed) - Activities (what the program does) - Outputs (countable products/services) - Short-term outcomes (1-6 months) - Intermediate outcomes (6-18 months) - Long-term impact (3-5 years) For each component, provide 3-5 specific items. Make sure the causal chain is logical — each level should clearly lead to the next.

Important: AI gives you a starting point. You refine it based on your knowledge of the community, evidence from the literature, and feedback from stakeholders.

Here's your first AI demo for the week. This prompt asks the AI to draft logic model components for your specific program. The key is in the last instruction — "make sure the causal chain is logical." AI is actually pretty good at generating plausible logic model components, but it tends to be generic. Your job is to take the output and make it specific to your context. Does the AI's list of inputs match what you actually have or can realistically obtain? Are the outcomes evidence-based — does the literature support the claim that your activities lead to these outcomes? Use AI to get the structure right, then apply your own expertise and the research to make it rigorous. I'd recommend running this prompt, reviewing the output, then revising it before you put it in your assignment.

Design a Survey

Draft survey questions for program participants, then implement in Google Forms (free).

ChatGPT / Claude PromptI need to create a brief survey (10-15 questions) for participants in my nonprofit program: [description]. My evaluation questions are: 1. [Your evaluation question 1] 2. [Your evaluation question 2] 3. [Your evaluation question 3] For each evaluation question, draft 2-4 survey items using: - Likert scale (strongly disagree to strongly agree) - Short-answer for qualitative data - Demographic questions (age, gender, zip code) Keep language at an 8th grade reading level. Avoid double-barreled or leading questions.

Next step: Copy the questions into Google Forms — free, accessible, and it auto-generates a spreadsheet for analysis.

This second demo shows how to use AI to draft survey instruments. The prompt is structured around your evaluation questions — which means you need to have those questions clear before you use this. The AI will generate draft survey items, but you need to check them carefully. Are the Likert scale anchors appropriate? Are the questions actually measuring what you think they're measuring? Are they written at an accessible reading level for your target population? Once you're satisfied with the questions, put them into Google Forms. It's free, it's easy to share, and it automatically creates a Google Sheets spreadsheet with responses. For your assignment, you don't need to actually administer the survey — but you should describe the instrument in your data collection plan. Having a draft survey ready shows you've thought concretely about implementation.

This week's website update is to add a Programs page. Think of this as the public-facing translation of your evaluation framework. Your assignment goes into technical detail — logic models, evaluation questions, data collection plans. Your website should tell the same story but in language that resonates with donors, community members, and potential partners. Lead with the problem your program addresses. Explain your theory of change in plain language. List the outcomes you expect — not in evaluation jargon, but in concrete terms that a prospective donor would find compelling. "We help youth stay in school and build relationships with caring adults" is more powerful on a website than "We improve school attendance rates and self-efficacy scores." Same substance, different framing.

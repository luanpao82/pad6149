Foundations

What Is Nonprofit Capacity?

Worth Ch. 7 (pg. 198–208)

Organizational capacity = the ability to fulfill your mission effectively and sustain that ability over time.

Capacity AreaWhat It Includes
FinancialRevenue diversification, reserves, budgeting systems, financial reporting
Human ResourcesStaff skills, volunteer management, training, succession planning
InfrastructureTechnology, facilities, data systems, administrative processes
ProgrammaticProgram design, evaluation, quality assurance, evidence base
Networks & PartnershipsCross-sector relationships, coalition membership, community connections

Capacity is not a fixed state — it's a continuum. Organizations build capacity incrementally as they grow from startup to mature institutions.

Worth's Chapter 7 defines organizational capacity in five interconnected areas. Financial capacity goes beyond having money — it's about having diversified revenue streams, adequate reserves, and the financial management systems to track and report accurately. Human resource capacity includes not just having staff, but having staff with the right skills, and systems for recruiting, training, and retaining them. Infrastructure capacity is often overlooked by new nonprofits — technology, data systems, office space, administrative processes. Programmatic capacity is what we covered in Week 10 with your evaluation frameworks — the ability to design, deliver, and evaluate effective programs. And network capacity — your relationships, partnerships, and coalition memberships — is a form of organizational capital that many funders now explicitly recognize. The key insight is that capacity is a continuum. You don't wake up one day with a fully capacitated organization. You build it deliberately, over time, and you prioritize based on where your gaps are most critical.
Research

Why Engage in Capacity Building?

Millesen, Carman & Bies (2010)

Understanding the incentives that drive capacity building.

External Drivers

  • Funder requirements and grants specifically for capacity building
  • Accreditation standards
  • Government contract compliance
  • Competitive pressure from peer organizations
  • Community expectations for professionalism

Internal Drivers

  • Board and leadership commitment to growth
  • Staff desire for professional development
  • Organizational learning culture
  • Recognition of gaps between current and needed capacity
  • Strategic plan goals requiring new capabilities

Key finding: Organizations driven primarily by external pressures tend to build capacity that satisfies funders. Organizations driven by internal motivation tend to build capacity that serves their mission.

Millesen, Carman, and Bies investigated why nonprofits engage in capacity building, and they found two distinct sets of drivers. External drivers include funder requirements — many foundations now offer capacity building grants or require grantees to demonstrate organizational development plans. Government contracts may require certain infrastructure. And competitive pressure matters — if your peer organizations are professionalizing, you risk falling behind. Internal drivers are about organizational will — board commitment, staff aspirations, a genuine culture of learning and improvement. The most important finding is that the motivation matters for what gets built. Externally driven capacity building tends to produce what funders want to see — better financial reporting, outcome measurement systems, strategic plans. Internally driven capacity building tends to produce what the organization actually needs — could be the same things, but chosen for the right reasons. For your nonprofits, think about both. What will funders expect? And what do you genuinely need to fulfill your mission?

Collaboration as Capacity

Guo & Acar (2005) — Understanding inter-organizational collaboration

Your partners are part of your capacity. Three theoretical lenses:

TheoryWhy Organizations CollaborateImplication
Resource DependencyYou need resources you don't have — funding, expertise, access, legitimacyPartnerships fill resource gaps without building everything in-house
Institutional TheoryFunders and regulators expect collaboration; it signals legitimacyPartnerships demonstrate community embeddedness to stakeholders
Network TheoryPosition in a network determines access to information, influence, and opportunitiesWell-connected organizations learn faster and adapt better

Remember Week 3? You identified cross-sector partners. Those partnerships are not just nice to have — they are a core capacity of your organization. Include them in your capacity building plan.

Guo and Acar's 2005 study brings three theoretical frameworks to explain why nonprofits collaborate. Resource dependency theory says organizations collaborate because they need resources they can't generate alone — money, expertise, access to populations, political legitimacy. Institutional theory says collaboration happens because the environment expects it — funders want to see partnerships, government agencies require them, and organizations that collaborate are seen as more legitimate. Network theory says your position in a web of relationships determines what information, opportunities, and influence you can access. All three perspectives are useful for your capacity building plan. Think back to Week 3 when you identified cross-sector partners. Those relationships are not separate from your organizational capacity — they ARE your organizational capacity. A startup nonprofit with strong partnerships has more effective capacity than a larger organization working in isolation. When you write your capacity building plan, include partnership development as a capacity area.

Adaptive Capacity

Strichman, Bickel & Marshood (2008) — Israeli social change nonprofits

How do organizations adapt to changing environments?

  • Resilience: The ability to absorb shocks — funding cuts, leadership transitions, policy changes — without losing core functions
  • Flexibility: Willingness to modify programs, structures, and strategies when the environment demands it
  • Learning orientation: Organizations that systematically reflect on their experience and apply lessons to future decisions
  • Environmental scanning: Continuously monitoring the political, economic, and social landscape for threats and opportunities

Adaptive capacity is not the same as capacity. An organization can be highly capacitated but rigid — unable to respond when conditions change. Adaptability is the meta-capacity that makes all other capacities useful in a dynamic world.

Strichman, Bickel, and Marshood studied social change nonprofits in Israel — organizations operating in an extremely volatile political and social environment. They found that the organizations that thrived were not necessarily the largest or best-funded, but the most adaptive. Adaptive capacity has four components. Resilience is about absorbing shocks without breaking down. Flexibility is about willingness to change course when the data or the environment tells you to. Learning orientation means you don't just do things — you reflect on what worked, what didn't, and why. And environmental scanning means you're paying attention to what's happening around you, not just inside your organization. The crucial insight is that adaptive capacity is a meta-capacity — it's what makes all your other capacities useful. You can have great financial systems, strong programs, and excellent staff, but if your organization can't adapt when conditions change — a major funder pulls out, a new policy shifts the landscape, a pandemic hits — all that capacity becomes brittle. Build adaptability into your organizational DNA from the start.
Change Management

Managing Organizational Change

Gottlieb (2009) — Creating futures requires managing transitions

Capacity building IS change management. Every new system, process, or partnership requires people to do something differently.

  • Resistance is normal: People resist change not because they're difficult, but because change threatens routines, relationships, and identity
  • Communication is foundational: People can't support what they don't understand. Over-communicate the why, the what, and the how
  • Stakeholder engagement: Those affected by change should participate in designing it. Top-down change breeds resentment; participatory change builds ownership
  • Phased implementation: Don't try to change everything at once. Sequence your capacity building priorities and celebrate incremental wins

Gottlieb's insight: Transformative change is not about fixing problems — it's about creating new possibilities. Frame capacity building as building toward a vision, not fixing deficiencies.

Gottlieb's final chapters — pages 279 through 324 — connect capacity building to change management, and this is where the rubber meets the road. Every capacity building initiative is fundamentally a change initiative. When you implement a new CRM system, you're asking staff to change how they work. When you restructure your board committees, you're asking board members to change how they govern. When you add new programs, you're asking the organization to stretch. Resistance is the natural human response to change, and effective leaders anticipate it. Communication is the single most important tool — people need to understand not just what's changing but why, and they need to see how the change connects to the mission they care about. Gottlieb adds an important reframe: don't position capacity building as fixing what's wrong. Position it as building toward what's possible. That's the difference between a deficit mindset and an abundance mindset, and it affects how people experience the change process.
Workbook

Capacity Building Plan

What infrastructure and systems does your organization need to scale from Year 1 to Year 3?

Technology

  • CRM / donor management
  • Website and digital presence
  • Data collection and analysis
  • Communication tools

Facilities

  • Office space needs
  • Program delivery space
  • Equipment and supplies
  • Accessibility requirements

Funding Pipeline

  • Grant readiness
  • Individual donor cultivation
  • Earned revenue potential
  • Reserve building plan

People & Partners

  • Staff development plan
  • Volunteer recruitment
  • Board development
  • Partnership cultivation

Think growth: What do you need in Year 1 to launch? What do you need by Year 3 to sustain and scale?

Your workbook task this week is to develop a capacity building plan organized around four areas: technology, facilities, funding pipeline, and people and partners. For each area, I want you to think about two time horizons: Year 1 — what's the minimum viable capacity you need to launch? And Year 3 — what do you need to sustain your programs and potentially grow? For technology, most startups need at minimum a basic CRM for donor and constituent management, a functional website, and some way to collect and analyze program data. By Year 3, you'll need more sophisticated systems. For facilities, think about what your programs actually require — do you need physical space, or can you operate virtually or in shared spaces initially? For your funding pipeline, this connects directly to your fundraising strategy from Week 8 — what capacity do you need to implement that strategy? And for people and partners, think about staff hiring, volunteer systems, board development, and the partnerships from Week 3. This is about realistic planning, not wish-listing. Be specific about what you need and when.

Part 1 · Theory

Three Sectors, One Ecosystem

Government

Policy, regulation, funding (grants & contracts), public accountability

Business

Capital, expertise, CSR, sponsorships, market-based solutions

Nonprofit

Mission-driven service, advocacy, community trust, volunteer labor

Key insight (Worth, Ch. 8): Your nonprofit will not operate in isolation. Every organization exists within a web of cross-sector relationships that shape its strategy, funding, and legitimacy.

Think about your capstone nonprofit — which sector relationships matter most?

Worth Chapter 8 lays out this fundamental idea: nonprofits don't exist in a vacuum. They are embedded in relationships with government and business. Government provides funding and sets the rules. Business provides capital, partnerships, and expertise. The nonprofit sector fills gaps that neither government nor business addresses effectively. As you build your capstone nonprofit, you need to think strategically about these relationships from day one.
Part 1

Government-Nonprofit Partnerships

Mechanisms of collaboration:

  • Grants — competitive funding for specific programs
  • Contracts — fee-for-service delivery on behalf of government
  • Policy advocacy — nonprofits as voice of constituents
  • Co-production — joint program design and implementation

Tensions & risks:

  • Mission drift from funder priorities
  • Administrative burden & compliance costs
  • Revenue dependency on a single source
  • Loss of advocacy independence

Gazley & Brudney (2007): Government-nonprofit partnerships can be effective but are often asymmetric — government holds the purse strings, and nonprofits bear the risk of shifting political priorities.

See also: Dicke & Ott (1999) on accountability challenges in contracting; Smith (2008) on the evolving contract regime.

Gazley and Brudney's 2007 study is essential reading here. They found that while both government and nonprofit managers generally view their partnerships positively, the relationships are often unequal. Government sets the terms. Nonprofits adapt. Dicke and Ott raise the accountability problem: when government outsources service delivery, who is really accountable to the public? Smith's work traces how the contracting regime has evolved and what it means for nonprofit autonomy. For your capstone nonprofit, the question is: will you seek government funding? If so, what are the trade-offs?
Part 1

Business-Nonprofit Relations

Types of engagement:

  • Corporate philanthropy — direct donations, foundation grants
  • Sponsorships — event/program support for brand visibility
  • Cause marketing — sales-linked donations (e.g., "buy one, give one")
  • Pro bono services — professional expertise at no cost
  • Social enterprise partnerships — joint ventures for impact

Watch out for:

  • "Greenwashing" or "causewashing" — brand use without real impact
  • Values misalignment (e.g., tobacco company funding health nonprofit)
  • Over-reliance on a single corporate partner
  • Loss of organizational identity

Young (2002): Business influence on nonprofits is pervasive and complex. The "businesslike" ethos can improve efficiency but risks displacing mission-centered values with market logic.

Young's 2002 work examines how business thinking has penetrated the nonprofit sector. On one hand, business partnerships bring resources, expertise, and visibility. On the other hand, when nonprofits adopt too much of a business mindset, they risk losing what makes them distinctive — their mission focus and public trust. Worth Chapter 11 extends this discussion to social enterprise and earned revenue. For your capstone nonprofit, think carefully: what kind of corporate partnerships make sense, and where would you draw the line?
Part 1

Cross-sector Collaboration Models

Young (2000) identifies three models of government-nonprofit relations:

Supplementary

Nonprofits fill gaps where government falls short. They provide services government cannot or will not offer.

Example: food banks supplementing SNAP benefits

Complementary

Nonprofits deliver services on behalf of government. Partners in implementation through contracts and grants.

Example: Head Start programs run by nonprofits

Adversarial

Nonprofits challenge government through advocacy, lobbying, and public pressure. Watchdog and reform role.

Example: ACLU suing government agencies

Your capstone question: Which model(s) describe your nonprofit's relationship with government? With business? Most organizations operate in more than one model simultaneously.

Young's 2000 framework is one of the most cited in our field. The supplementary model is the classic market-failure argument — nonprofits exist because government cannot satisfy all demands. The complementary model reflects the partnership reality — government funds, nonprofits deliver. The adversarial model is about advocacy and accountability. Most real-world nonprofits operate across all three models simultaneously. For your workbook task, I want you to think about where your capstone nonprofit fits. It's rarely just one model.

Don't just say "the government." Say "Orange County Department of Social Services" or "Florida Department of Children and Families." Specificity shows you've done your homework and are thinking strategically about your nonprofit's ecosystem.

Step-by-Step: Google Sites

1
Go to sites.google.com and click the "+" button to create a blank site. Sign in with your UCF or personal Google account.
2
Name your site with your nonprofit's name. This becomes the page title and the URL slug (e.g., sites.google.com/view/your-nonprofit).
3
Add a hero section: Insert a large banner image + your mission statement as the headline. Use the title text area for your tagline.
4
Create pages: In the right panel under "Pages," add: Home, About, Board, Team, Programs, Donate, Advocacy. Content comes later — set up the structure now.
5
Publish: Click "Publish" in the top right. Choose a clean URL. Share your link with the class!

Time estimate: 15–20 minutes to set up the basic structure. You can refine design and content throughout the week.

Let me walk through this step by step. First, go to sites.google.com. You'll see a gallery of templates — ignore those and click the blank "+" button. Name it with your nonprofit's name. Step 3 is the most important for first impressions: add a large hero image and put your mission statement front and center. For images, use Unsplash.com for free professional photos. Step 4: create the page structure. You don't need all the content yet — just create placeholder pages. Step 5: publish it. You now have a live URL you can share. Total time: about 15-20 minutes for the basic structure.
AI Demo

Generate Your Website Copy

Use Claude or ChatGPT to draft professional content for your site. Try this prompt:

I'm building a website for my nonprofit [Name].
Our mission is [mission statement].
Our target population is [population].

Write website copy for:
1. Homepage hero section (2-3 sentences, inspiring)
2. About Us page (200 words)
3. Our Mission section (100 words)

Tone: professional, warm, action-oriented.
Avoid jargon. Write for a general audience.

Critical step: AI gives you a starting point. You must edit, personalize, and verify every word. Your voice and your nonprofit's identity must come through. Do not submit generic AI output.

AI is a powerful drafting tool. Go to claude.ai or chatgpt.com and paste this prompt, filling in your nonprofit's specifics. The output will give you a solid first draft. But here is the critical point: you must personalize it. AI-generated copy is generic by nature. Your job is to inject your nonprofit's unique voice, your community's specific needs, and your personal passion. I will be able to tell if you submit raw AI output without editing. Use AI as a starting point, not a finished product.

Visual identity matters more than you think. A professional logo and consistent colors signal legitimacy. Canva's free tier is more than sufficient.

Week 1

Welcome to the Capstone

This is the final course in your MNM journey. You've spent the program learning about nonprofits — now you're going to build one.

The "Build Your Nonprofit" Concept: Over 15 weeks, you will design a complete nonprofit organization from the ground up — mission, governance, budget, programs, website, and a final pitch.

Every assignment in this course adds a real component to your organization. By the end of the semester, you'll have a professional-quality organizational portfolio that demonstrates your mastery of nonprofit management.

Let me be clear about what makes this capstone different. This isn't a course where you write disconnected papers about nonprofit theory. Everything you produce in this class contributes to a single, cohesive project: your nonprofit organization. Think of it as an extended exercise in organizational design. You've taken courses in governance, finance, fundraising, program evaluation — now you're going to integrate all of that knowledge into one comprehensive deliverable. The portfolio you build can serve as a writing sample, a job interview talking point, or even the foundation for an actual organization if you choose to pursue it.
Philosophy

Two Ideas That Drive This Course

1. Learning by Doing

You will build a nonprofit organization from scratch. Each week's deliverable becomes a real component — a board structure, a budget, a strategic plan, a website page.

This is applied learning at the graduate level. Theory matters, but execution is the capstone competency.

2. AI as Professional Competency

AI tools are required in this course, not prohibited. Nonprofit professionals are already using AI for grant writing, communications, and data analysis.

You will learn to use AI critically — as a collaborator, not a replacement for your judgment.

I want to set the tone right away with two foundational ideas. First, this is a doing course. You are not going to write a 20-page paper analyzing someone else's nonprofit. You are going to design your own. That means every week you're producing something tangible — a governance document, a budget spreadsheet, a marketing plan, a website. Second, and this may surprise you: I require you to use AI tools in this course. The nonprofit sector is already adopting AI for grant writing, donor communications, program evaluation, and more. If we're preparing you for the real world, we need to prepare you to use these tools responsibly and critically. You'll document your AI use in process logs so we can have honest conversations about what works and what doesn't.

Key Deliverable

Your Nonprofit Website

Throughout the semester, you will build and maintain a live website for your nonprofit organization.

  • Week 3: Launch your website with mission, About Us, and initial branding
  • Ongoing: Each major deliverable becomes a page or section on the site
  • Week 14: Final portfolio — a complete, professional organizational website

By the end of the course, your website will include:

  • Mission and vision statements
  • Board of directors page
  • Programs and services
  • Strategic plan summary
  • Budget overview
  • Fundraising/donation page
  • Advocacy and policy positions
  • Impact and evaluation data
The website is the backbone of this course. Think of it as both a deliverable and a portfolio container. In Week 3, you'll launch a basic site with your nonprofit's name, mission, and branding. From there, each assignment feeds into the website. When you write bylaws, those get summarized on a governance page. When you create a budget, a summary goes on the site. When you design programs, they get their own page. By Week 14, you'll have a fully built-out organizational website that looks and reads like a real nonprofit. You can use any platform — Wix, WordPress, Squarespace, Google Sites, GitHub Pages, or code it yourself. I don't care about the tool. I care about the quality of the content and the professionalism of the presentation.

Service Learning

Mentor Interview (3 Parts)

A required service learning component: connect with a real nonprofit practitioner and learn from their experience.

The Three Parts

  • Part 1 — Concept Statement (Wk 2): Identify your mentor and explain why you chose them
  • Part 2 — Interview Plan (Wk 6): Develop questions aligned to your nonprofit's needs
  • Part 3 — Report (Wk 10): Conduct the interview, reflect on insights, apply lessons

Why This Matters

  • Real-world practitioners have knowledge that textbooks don't capture
  • Networking is a core professional skill in the sector
  • Insights from your mentor should inform your nonprofit's design
The service learning component is a three-part assignment spread across the semester. You'll identify a nonprofit professional — someone working in a leadership role at an organization related to your focus area — and conduct an informational interview with them. Part 1, due in Week 2, is a brief concept statement: who is your mentor, what organization do they work for, and why did you choose them? Part 2, in Week 6, is your interview plan with thoughtful questions. And Part 3, in Week 10, is the full interview report with reflections on what you learned and how it applies to your nonprofit. This assignment matters because you're building something from scratch, and real practitioners have insights that no textbook can give you. Start thinking now about who you might want to interview.

This course requires the use of AI tools. You will document your AI use in an AI Process Log for every major deliverable.

Recommended Free Tools

  • Canva AI — design, branding
  • Gamma.app — presentations

The Rule: You must author, evaluate, and own all work. AI is a tool — not the author. Use it to enhance your thinking, not replace it.

Let me be direct about AI. I am not going to pretend AI doesn't exist, and I'm not going to ban it. Instead, I'm going to teach you to use it like a professional. Every major assignment will include an AI Process Log where you document what tools you used, what prompts you wrote, and how you evaluated and revised the AI's output. This is important for two reasons. First, it teaches you to be transparent and reflective about AI use — which is an emerging ethical standard in our field. Second, it creates accountability. If you just copy-paste AI output without evaluation, that will be evident in your process log and in the quality of your work. The tools listed here are all free. You don't need to spend money. But you do need to engage seriously with at least one AI tool throughout the semester.

Guidance

How to Choose Your Cause

Ask yourself: What community problem keeps you up at night?

Example Focus Areas

  • Youth education and mentorship
  • Housing insecurity and homelessness
  • Mental health access
  • Immigration and refugee services
  • Environmental justice
  • Arts and cultural access
  • Food security and nutrition
  • Workforce development

Tips for Deciding

  • Think local. What needs exist in your community?
  • Think specific. "Youth education" is broad — "STEM mentoring for first-gen middle schoolers in Orlando" is a concept.
  • Think feasible. Could this realistically be a small nonprofit?
  • Think personal. Passion sustains you through 15 weeks of work.
Choosing your cause is the most important decision you'll make this week, so let me offer some guidance. The examples on the left are common nonprofit focus areas, but these are starting points, not answers. The key is specificity. If you say "I want to help kids," that's not a nonprofit concept yet. If you say "I want to create an after-school STEM program for first-generation middle school students in the Pine Hills neighborhood of Orlando," now we're talking. Think about your community — where are the gaps? Think about your own experience — what populations or issues have you encountered professionally or personally? And think practically. You're designing an organization that could, in theory, actually exist. Keep the scope manageable. A small, focused nonprofit is more compelling than a vague, everything-for-everyone organization.
AI Demo

Brainstorming Your Concept with AI

Here's an example of productive AI use for this week's assignment:

Sample PromptI'm a graduate student creating a nonprofit organization focused on food insecurity among college students. Help me brainstorm: - 3 mission statement options - Target population details - 3 potential program ideas - 5 creative organization names

Good AI Use vs. Bad AI Use

Good: Use AI to brainstorm, then select and refine with your own judgment. Document what you changed and why.

Bad: Copy the AI's first output directly into your submission. No evaluation, no revision, no original thought.

Let me show you what productive AI use looks like in practice. Say you're interested in food insecurity among college students. You might prompt ChatGPT or Claude with something like this: give me mission statement options, target population details, program ideas, and name suggestions. The AI will generate useful raw material. But here's the critical step: you evaluate it. Maybe the AI suggests a name that's already taken. Maybe the mission statement is too generic. Maybe one of the program ideas doesn't fit your local context. Your job is to curate, refine, and make it yours. In your AI Process Log, you'd note: I used Claude to brainstorm five organization names, selected "Knights Pantry Collective" as my working name because it reflects the UCF community, and modified the suggested mission statement to focus specifically on graduate students. That's the kind of critical engagement I'm looking for.

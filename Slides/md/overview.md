# Redesigning the MNM Capstone
*MNM Faculty & Board Briefing · Fall 2026*

PAD 6149 — Nonprofit Administration: *Build Your Nonprofit with AI*

Dr. Seongho An  
School of Public Administration  
University of Central Florida

> [!notes]
> Thank you for the time today. I want to walk you through a ground-up redesign of PAD 6149, our MNM capstone. The redesign takes effect in Fall 2026. My goal in the next fifteen minutes is simple: explain what has changed, why, and what students will walk out of this course with that they did not before. I'll save questions for the end.

---

## The Capstone We Inherited
*Starting Point*

Under its current design, PAD 6149 was organized around **three retrospective activities**:

- **Discussion Board Participation — 50%.** Students posted ~1,000 words per week reacting to readings.
- **ePortfolio Assignment — 30%.** Students uploaded prior MNM coursework with short reflection papers organized by NASPAA competency.
- **Leadership Case Study — 20%.** A single written case analysis.

AI tools were explicitly **prohibited**: "The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools… is not permitted in this class."

> [!notes]
> This is where we were. The course was effectively a review semester: students looked backward at work they had already done, reflected on it, and posted in discussion boards. That design had integrity when it was built — the ePortfolio was the intended synthesis artifact. But it leaves students with no new, forward-facing deliverable. And the explicit AI prohibition, written in 2024, is now out of step with what every practicing nonprofit leader actually does on the job.

---

## One Sentence
*The Redesign*

> [!quote] Core Design Principle, PAD 6149 (Fall 2026)
> Students do not write *about* nonprofit management.
> They **build a nonprofit**.

Every weekly deliverable in the redesigned course adds a real component to a single cohesive organization — governance documents, budget, staffing plan, strategic plan, program design, fundraising strategy, advocacy plan, and a public-facing website. By Week 14, the student has produced a complete nonprofit blueprint.

> [!notes]
> If you take only one idea from this briefing, let it be this one. The capstone is no longer a retrospective. It is a design studio. Students spend fifteen weeks building one nonprofit organization from scratch — a real one, documented publicly. Every textbook concept, every prior assignment they've written for other MNM courses, gets reactivated in service of that one organization. It's the difference between writing a memoir and producing a working prototype.

We removed the discussion boards, which accounted for half the prior grade, because the signal-to-noise ratio on asynchronous 1,000-word posts was poor. We replaced the retrospective ePortfolio with a forward-facing public website. And we reversed the AI policy — I'll explain the rationale in a moment.

## Two Ideas Driving the Redesign
*Design Philosophy*

<div class="two-col">
  <div>
    <h3>1. Execution is the capstone competency</h3>
    <p>Graduate students in a professional program don't need more exposure to concepts. They need repeated practice <strong>producing</strong> the artifacts a nonprofit leader actually produces — bylaws, budgets, logic models, pitch decks.</p>
  </div>
  <div>
    <h3>2. AI fluency is a professional competency</h3>
    <p>Nonprofit managers already use AI for grant writing, donor communications, program evaluation, and strategic planning. Prohibiting it leaves graduates unprepared. Requiring it — with a documented ethical framework — prepares them.</p>
  </div>
</div>

> [!highlight]
> Both ideas point in the same direction: a capstone measured by what students can **do**, not what they can recall.

> [!notes]
> The redesign rests on two claims. The first is pedagogical: at the end of a professional master's degree, the right evidence of mastery is a produced artifact, not a recalled fact. The second is environmental: our graduates enter a sector that already uses AI daily, so we need to teach them to use it responsibly rather than pretend it doesn't exist. These two ideas are complementary — building a real nonprofit is the natural context in which to learn applied AI use, because both require judgment, iteration, and critique rather than memorization.

    <ul>
      <li>Grounds abstract course concepts in practitioner reality</li>

      <li>Reconnects MNM students to the Central Florida nonprofit ecosystem</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

> [!highlight]
> This is the **only new component** not adapted from prior MNM coursework. It is service learning in the formal sense — applied engagement with the community the program serves.

> [!notes]
> The mentor interview is the one piece of the redesigned course that isn't pulled from prior MNM coursework — and it's deliberately the piece that puts students in contact with practitioners. Every student identifies a working nonprofit leader, prepares a structured interview, conducts it, and writes up what they learned. This does three things. It grounds the semester in real practice rather than abstraction. It gives students a networking artifact they can cite in job interviews. And it quietly rebuilds the program's visible presence in the Central Florida nonprofit community, because each semester adds a new cohort of students reaching out to local leaders.

## AI as Required Professional Competency
*AI Policy*

<div class="compare">
  <div class="pane old">
    <h3>Current Policy </h3>
    <p>"The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools, via website, app, or any other access, is <strong>not permitted</strong> in this class."</p>
  </div>
  <div class="pane new">
    <h3>New Policy (2026)</h3>
    <p>AI use is <strong>required</strong>. Students document their AI use on every major deliverable and are graded on the quality of their judgment, not the volume of their output.</p>
  </div>
</div>

### The Four Rules

- **Authorship.** Students must author, direct, critically evaluate, and own all AI-assisted work.
- **No raw output.** Submitting unrevised AI text is treated as academic dishonesty.
- **Disclosure.** AI-generated images that could be mistaken for photographs must be labeled.
- **Privacy.** No real donor, client, or personnel data in AI tools — fictional only.

All required tools have free tiers. No paid subscriptions.

> [!notes]
> This is the policy change I expect most discussion on. The rationale: practicing nonprofit leaders are already using AI for grant drafting, donor communications, program evaluation, and social media. A graduate who cannot use these tools ethically and critically is already behind. Prohibiting AI in a capstone course in 2026 would be the equivalent of prohibiting spreadsheets in a finance course in 2005. The policy has guardrails — authorship, disclosure, privacy, and an ethical framework that treats AI as a tool the student directs, not an author that replaces them. We use only free-tier tools, so there is no financial barrier for students.

> [!highlight]
> The redesign preserves the competency coverage of the ePortfolio but moves the evidence from *reflection on past work* to *demonstration in new work*.

> [!notes]
> Because PAD 6149 is the capstone in a NASPAA-accredited program, the redesign had to preserve competency coverage. It does. Every one of the five universal competencies has a specific anchor deliverable in the new course — and in every case, the evidence is stronger than it was under the ePortfolio model. The old model asked students to reflect on past competency. The new model asks them to demonstrate it, freshly, on an organization they designed. For accreditation review, the artifacts produced in this course are far more defensible.

*Program Value*

<div class="two-col">
  <div>
    <h3>Differentiation</h3>
    <p>Most MNM and MPA capstones are reflective. A design-studio capstone where students build and publish a real nonprofit is a distinctive recruitment and reputation asset.</p>
    <h3>Recruitment</h3>
    <p>Every cohort produces visible alumni work — live websites and pitch videos — that can be showcased to prospective students and during accreditation review.</p>
  </div>

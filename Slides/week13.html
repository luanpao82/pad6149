Effectiveness

Measuring Nonprofit Effectiveness:
Mission Impossible?

Sawhill & Williamson (2001) asked a deceptively simple question: Can nonprofits measure their success in achieving their mission?

  • The problem: Nonprofit missions are often aspirational and long-term — "end homelessness," "promote justice," "protect the environment"
  • Three levels of measurement: Mission-level impact (hardest), program-level outcomes (manageable), organizational capacity (easiest)
  • Most nonprofits default to measuring activity (outputs) rather than impact (outcomes) — because impact is expensive and hard to attribute
  • Their solution: Organizations should develop mission-aligned metrics even if they cannot measure ultimate impact directly

For your nonprofit: What metrics would show your organization is moving toward its mission? Not just "number served" — but what changed?

Sawhill and Williamson's 2001 article in Nonprofit Management and Leadership is one of the most cited pieces on this topic. Their argument is that measuring mission-level impact is genuinely difficult — sometimes impossible — for nonprofits working on complex social problems. But that does not excuse organizations from trying. They propose a hierarchy: at the base, you measure organizational capacity — are you financially healthy, well-governed, adequately staffed? In the middle, you measure program outcomes — did participants achieve specific results? At the top, you try to measure mission impact — is the larger problem getting better because of your work? Most organizations stay at the bottom because it is easiest. The challenge for you, as you build your nonprofit, is to think about what genuine effectiveness looks like beyond counting outputs.
Research

Doing Things Right:
Herman & Renz (2004)

A panel study of local nonprofits in the Kansas City area — one of the few longitudinal studies of nonprofit effectiveness.

Management Effectiveness

Quality of internal operations, planning, and leadership

Program Effectiveness

Outcomes achieved by programs and services

Stakeholder Satisfaction

Board, donors, clients, community members — all evaluate differently

Financial Health

Revenue diversification, reserves, efficiency ratios

Key finding: No single metric captures effectiveness. Organizations rated highly on one dimension were not necessarily rated highly on others. Effectiveness is multi-dimensional.

Herman and Renz published this in Public Administration Review in 2004. What makes it notable is the longitudinal design — they studied the same organizations over time, which is rare in nonprofit research. Their four dimensions of effectiveness show that an organization can be financially healthy but have poor programs, or have excellent programs but weak governance. The implication is that you cannot reduce effectiveness to a single number or rating. This is why systems like Charity Navigator's star ratings have been criticized — they tend to overweight financial efficiency at the expense of program quality. For your nonprofit, think about which dimensions matter most to your stakeholders. A funder may care about financial health; a client may care about program quality; your board may care about organizational reputation. You need to manage all of these simultaneously.
Research

Nine Theses on Nonprofit Effectiveness:
Herman & Renz (2008)

Building on a decade of research, Herman & Renz offered nine propositions to advance the field:

  1. Nonprofit organizational effectiveness is always comparative
  2. Effectiveness is multidimensional — it cannot be reduced to a single measure
  3. Effectiveness is related to board effectiveness, but the relationship is complex
  4. Organizations that use correct management practices are more likely to be effective
  5. Effectiveness is a social construction — stakeholders define it differently
  6. It is unlikely that there are universal best practices that always lead to effectiveness
  7. Effectiveness is responsive to the environment — context matters
  8. Organizational effectiveness is related to program effectiveness but they are not the same
  9. The more we study effectiveness, the more complex it becomes
This 2008 article in Nonprofit Management and Leadership is essentially Herman and Renz's summary statement after years of studying this question. Thesis 5 is the most important for our purposes: effectiveness is socially constructed. That means your donors, your board, your clients, your community, and your regulators may all define "effective" differently. A homeless shelter's clients define effectiveness as "I got a safe bed tonight." A donor defines it as "my money produced measurable outcomes." A policymaker defines it as "the homelessness rate went down." Your nonprofit must navigate all of these definitions. Thesis 6 is also critical — there are no universal best practices. What works in one context may not work in another. This is why strategic planning, which we covered in Week 9, matters so much: you have to understand your specific context and design accordingly.
Ethics

Ethics and Effectiveness

Malloy & Agarwal (2001)

  • Studied ethical climate in nonprofit organizations
  • Organizations with strong principled ethical climates demonstrated better decision-making
  • Ethical climate shapes how employees respond to dilemmas — rules-based vs. values-based
  • Values-driven nonprofits align ethics with mission, not just compliance

Rhode & Packel (2009)

  • Stanford Social Innovation Review piece on ethics in the nonprofit sector
  • Nonprofits are not immune to ethical failures — conflicts of interest, financial mismanagement, mission drift
  • Public trust is the sector's most valuable asset
  • Strong ethics → better governance → better outcomes → sustained effectiveness

The connection: You cannot be effective without being ethical. And you cannot be ethical without intentional structures — codes of conduct, transparent governance, accountability mechanisms.

These two readings connect ethics to effectiveness in complementary ways. Malloy and Agarwal, writing in NML in 2001, found that the ethical climate of an organization — the shared perceptions of what is ethically correct — directly influences how staff behave when facing difficult decisions. Organizations with principled climates, where decisions are guided by values and professional standards rather than self-interest or organizational politics, tend to perform better. Rhode and Packel, writing in SSIR in 2009, take a more practical perspective. They catalog the ethical challenges nonprofits face — executive compensation controversies, conflicts of interest on boards, donor influence on programming, financial opacity — and argue that the nonprofit sector's legitimacy depends on maintaining ethical standards that exceed the for-profit sector. For your nonprofit, this means building ethics into your governance structure from day one. Your board's code of conduct, your conflict of interest policy, your financial transparency — these are not just compliance exercises. They are the foundation of your effectiveness.
Advocacy

Nonprofit Advocacy:
Why Policy Matters

Your nonprofit does not just serve clients — it can change the systems that create the problems your clients face.

  • Advocacy is legal. 501(c)(3) organizations can and should advocate for policy change
  • Lobbying has limits — direct lobbying (contacting legislators about specific legislation) is restricted, not prohibited
  • Grassroots advocacy — educating the public, mobilizing constituents, issuing reports — has no expenditure limits
  • Public education — research, data, issue framing — is not lobbying at all
Activity Type Limit
Publishing research on an issue Public education No limit
Urging the public to contact legislators Grassroots lobbying Subject to expenditure test
Meeting with a legislator about a bill Direct lobbying Subject to expenditure test
Hosting a candidate forum (nonpartisan) Voter education No limit
Endorsing a candidate Prohibited Absolutely prohibited for 501(c)(3)
This is one of the most misunderstood areas of nonprofit law. Many nonprofit leaders believe that 501(c)(3) organizations cannot engage in advocacy at all. That is wrong. The restriction is on excessive lobbying — meaning direct communication with legislators about specific legislation — and even that is permitted within limits. Under the 501(h) election, organizations can spend up to 20% of their first $500,000 on lobbying. Grassroots advocacy, public education, research, coalition building, media engagement — none of these are lobbying. Your advocacy plan should show that you understand these distinctions and can design a strategy that is both effective and legally compliant. Think about your prior coursework in PAD 6142 and PAD 5146 — you studied the policy process. Now you are applying it to your own organization.

C5: Participate in and contribute to the policy process

5–7 pages. Build on your prior coursework from PAD 6142 (Policy Advocacy Plan) and PAD 5146 (Policy Process Assignment).

Component Description
Issue Framing Define the policy problem your nonprofit addresses. Use data.
Policy Goal What specific policy change are you advocating for?
Stakeholder Map Who are the allies, opponents, and decision-makers?
Advocacy Strategy Tactics that are legal under 501(c)(3). Public education, grassroots, coalition.
Legislative Contact Plan How will you engage elected officials? At what level?
Public Communication Plan Media, social media, community engagement.
Evaluation How will you measure the success of your advocacy efforts?
This is your fifth and final competency assignment, worth 10 points. The advocacy plan should be 5 to 7 pages and should demonstrate that you can participate in and contribute to the policy process — NASPAA Competency 5. Each component builds on skills you have already developed. Issue framing draws on your mission and needs assessment. Stakeholder mapping draws on your governance and community analysis. The advocacy strategy requires you to understand the legal framework for 501(c)(3) advocacy. The legislative contact plan requires you to identify specific officials at the local, state, or federal level who have jurisdiction over your issue. And the evaluation section asks you to think about how you would know if your advocacy worked. This is not hypothetical — real nonprofits do this work every day. The National Council of Nonprofits, Independent Sector, and your state nonprofit association all provide resources for advocacy planning.

AI Demo: Draft a Policy Brief

Use Claude to draft a structured policy brief for your advocacy plan:

Claude PromptDraft a 2-page policy brief on [your issue] for [target audience — e.g., Florida state legislators]. Include: - Problem statement with current data and trends - Policy recommendation (specific, actionable) - Implementation considerations - Fiscal impact estimate - Supporting evidence from peer-reviewed research Tone: professional, nonpartisan, evidence-based. Organization: [your nonprofit name].
  • Review the output critically — verify all data points, check that recommendations are realistic
  • Revise for your voice — the brief should sound like your organization, not a generic AI output
  • Add local context — Florida-specific data, local examples, state legislative history
This AI demo shows you how to use Claude to draft a policy brief, which can be a component of or supplement to your advocacy plan. The key is the prompt structure — specifying the audience, the tone, and the required sections. A policy brief for a state legislator looks different from one written for a foundation or a community group. When you get the output, do not submit it as-is. Verify every data point. Check that the policy recommendation is actually feasible. Add Florida-specific context — legislators respond to local data, not national statistics. And make sure the voice matches your organization's brand. AI gives you a strong draft; you make it authentic and accurate.

AI Demo: Create an Advocacy One-Pager

Use Canva AI to design a one-page advocacy handout for your nonprofit:

What to Include

  • Your organization's name and logo
  • The key message — one sentence
  • 2–3 supporting data points
  • A clear call to action
  • Contact information

Design Principles

  • One page, front only
  • Large, scannable text
  • Your brand colors and fonts
  • Visual hierarchy: message → data → action
  • No jargon — designed for a legislator's aide who has 30 seconds

This one-pager can be included in your advocacy plan appendix and linked from your website's advocacy page.

The advocacy one-pager is one of the most practical tools in a nonprofit's policy toolkit. When you visit a legislator's office — or send materials to their staff — you need something that communicates your message in 30 seconds or less. Canva's AI tools can generate a layout for you, but you need to supply the content. The key message should be one sentence that a staffer could repeat to the legislator. The data should be specific and local — "In Orange County, 1 in 5 children experiences food insecurity" is stronger than "hunger is a national problem." The call to action should be concrete — "Support HB 1234" or "Fund the state nutrition assistance program at $X million." This is a real deliverable that could go on your website and into your advocacy plan.
Website

Website Update: Add Your Advocacy Page

Add an "Advocacy" or "Take Action" page to your nonprofit website.

  • Policy goals: What issues does your nonprofit care about at the policy level?
  • Key issues: Brief, accessible explanation of the problems and your position
  • How supporters can take action:
    • Contact their legislators (provide names, links, phone numbers)
    • Sign petitions or join coalitions
    • Attend public hearings or community events
    • Share your advocacy materials on social media
  • Resources: Link to your policy brief, one-pager, and relevant reports

Examples to study: Look at advocacy pages from organizations like the National Council of Nonprofits, Feeding America, or Habitat for Humanity. Notice how they make policy accessible to non-expert audiences.

This is your final major website addition. The advocacy page demonstrates that your nonprofit is not just a service provider — it is a voice for systemic change. A good advocacy page does three things: it educates visitors about the policy issues, it provides clear and specific actions they can take, and it positions your organization as a credible voice on the issue. Most of you have been building your websites week by week throughout the semester. After this addition, your site should have all the core pages: Home, About, Board, Team, Get Involved, Programs, Donate, Our Strategy, and now Advocacy.

Worth Ch. 10

Why Marketing Matters for Nonprofits

It's not selling — it's storytelling. Communicating mission, building trust, attracting support.

What Nonprofit Marketing Does

  • Communicates mission: Tells your story in a way that resonates with different audiences
  • Builds trust: Consistent, professional communication signals legitimacy
  • Attracts resources: Donors, volunteers, partners, and funders find you through marketing
  • Engages constituents: The people you serve need to know you exist and how to access your programs

Common Misconceptions

MythReality
"Marketing is for businesses"Every nonprofit markets — the question is whether you do it well or poorly
"It's expensive"Most effective nonprofit marketing is free or low-cost (social media, email, website)
"Our work speaks for itself"It doesn't — if people don't know about you, your impact is limited
Worth Chapter 10 makes the case that marketing is not a luxury for nonprofits — it's a necessity. Some nonprofit leaders are uncomfortable with the word "marketing" because it feels commercial. But marketing is simply communication with purpose. You're telling your story, building trust, and making it easy for people to support your work. The alternative — not marketing — means you're invisible. The best programs in the world don't matter if no one knows they exist. Every nonprofit markets, whether they call it that or not. The question is whether you're doing it intentionally and well, or accidentally and poorly.
Pope, Isely & Asamoa-Tutu (2009)

Developing a Marketing Strategy

An exploratory study of marketing practices in nonprofit organizations.

Market Segmentation

Who are your audiences? Not one monolithic "public" — segment by relationship to your organization:

  • Donors: Individual, corporate, foundation
  • Volunteers: Skilled, general, episodic
  • Beneficiaries: The people you serve
  • Partners: Other organizations, government

Positioning

What makes your organization distinct? Your positioning statement answers:

  • Who do we serve?
  • What need do we address?
  • How are we different from similar organizations?
  • Why should someone support us?

Branding

Your brand is not your logo — it's the feeling people have when they encounter your organization:

  • Visual identity (logo, colors, fonts)
  • Voice and tone
  • Consistency across all touchpoints
  • Trust and reputation over time
Pope, Isely, and Asamoa-Tutu's exploratory study found that many nonprofits approach marketing haphazardly — they post on social media when they remember, send newsletters irregularly, and don't think strategically about who they're trying to reach. A real marketing strategy starts with segmentation: who are your audiences? Donors are different from volunteers, who are different from the people you serve. Each group needs different messages through different channels. Then positioning: what makes you distinctive? In a crowded nonprofit landscape, you need to clearly articulate why someone should support your organization over others doing similar work. And finally, branding: the consistent visual and verbal identity that makes your organization recognizable and trustworthy.
Stater (2009)

Revenue Sources Shape Marketing

How your funding model determines your marketing strategy.

Funding Model Primary Audience Marketing Focus Key Channels
Grant-dependent Foundations, government agencies Outcomes data, program effectiveness, compliance track record Grant portals, reports, conferences, Candid profile
Donation-dependent Individual donors, community Emotional storytelling, impact narratives, donor stewardship Social media, email, events, website, direct mail
Earned income Customers, fee-for-service clients Value proposition, service quality, competitive pricing Website, SEO, advertising, partnerships, referrals
Diversified Multiple stakeholders Different messages for different audiences; unified brand All of the above, tailored by segment

For your workbook: Identify your nonprofit's likely funding model. How does that shape who you need to reach and what you need to communicate?

Stater's research reveals something important: your funding model determines your marketing strategy. A grant-dependent organization markets to program officers with data, outcomes, and compliance records. A donation-dependent organization markets to individual donors with emotional stories, impact narratives, and personal connection. An earned-income organization markets like a business — value proposition, service quality, competitive advantage. Most of your nonprofits will be diversified, which means you need multiple marketing approaches for different audiences, all tied together by a consistent brand identity. In your workbook, identify which funding model your nonprofit will primarily rely on and think about how that shapes your communication strategy.
Practical

Digital Marketing for Nonprofits

Free and low-cost tools every nonprofit should use.

Essential Channels

  • Website: Your digital home base. Every other channel drives traffic here.
  • Email newsletters: Highest ROI channel for nonprofits. Direct relationship with supporters.
  • Social media: Choose 2–3 platforms where your audience actually is. Quality over quantity.
  • SEO: When someone searches for your cause area, do they find you?

Free Tools

ToolUse
Canva (free tier)Graphics, social posts, flyers, presentations
Mailchimp (free tier)Email newsletters (up to 500 contacts)
Google for Nonprofits$10K/month in Google Ads, Google Workspace
Buffer / LaterSocial media scheduling
Google AnalyticsWebsite traffic and behavior tracking
Let's get practical about digital marketing. Your website is the hub — every other marketing effort drives people to your site. Email newsletters have the highest return on investment of any nonprofit marketing channel because they create a direct, owned relationship with supporters — you're not at the mercy of an algorithm. Social media matters, but be strategic: pick two or three platforms where your target audience actually spends time. Don't try to be everywhere. And SEO — search engine optimization — is critical: when someone in your community searches for the problem you're solving, your organization should appear. All of these tools are free or nearly free. Google for Nonprofits alone provides $10,000 per month in Google Ads credits, which is transformative for visibility.
Brand

Brand Identity Elements

These should be consistent across your website, documents, and communications.

Verbal Identity

  • Mission statement (final version this week — under 25 words)
  • Tagline (memorable, 5–8 words)
  • Voice/tone guide (professional but warm? Urgent? Empowering?)
  • Elevator pitch (30 seconds, conversational)

Visual Identity

  • Logo (create in Canva — keep it simple and scalable)
  • Color palette (2–3 primary colors + neutrals)
  • Typography (one heading font, one body font)
  • Photography style (real people? Illustrations? What mood?)

Application

  • Website (primary showcase)
  • Social media profiles
  • Letterhead and documents
  • Presentation templates
  • Event materials

The test: If someone sees your website, then your social media, then a printed flyer — do they instantly know it's the same organization? That's brand consistency.

Brand identity has two components: verbal and visual. Your verbal identity includes your mission statement — which you should finalize this week at under 25 words — a tagline, a voice and tone guide, and an elevator pitch. Your visual identity includes a logo, color palette, typography choices, and photography or imagery style. The key principle is consistency. Every touchpoint should feel like the same organization. When a donor visits your website and then sees your social media post, they should instantly recognize it as yours. This week in your workbook, you'll draft all of these elements. Use Canva to create a simple logo — don't overthink it; a clean wordmark with one accent color is better than a complicated design.
AI Demo

Finalize Your Mission Statement

Use Claude to refine your draft mission statement into a final, polished version.

Prompt:

"Here is my nonprofit's draft mission statement: [paste your current draft].

Help me refine it to be:
• Concise (under 25 words)
• Action-oriented (starts with a verb)
• Specific about who we serve and what we do
• Free of jargon and buzzwords

Give me 3 options, each with a different emphasis."

Before (typical draft)

"Our organization seeks to provide comprehensive support services and educational programming to underserved youth in the Central Florida area to help them achieve their full potential."

38 words. Vague. Passive. Jargon-heavy.

After (refined)

"Equipping Central Florida youth with mentorship, tutoring, and career skills to thrive beyond high school."

15 words. Active verb. Specific. Clear audience and services.

Here's a practical AI demo for refining your mission statement. Most first drafts are too long, too vague, and too full of jargon. The prompt asks Claude for three options with different emphases so you can choose the direction that feels right, then customize further. Look at the before and after on screen. The draft is 38 words of vague aspirational language. The refined version is 15 words that tell you exactly who the organization serves and what it does. Your mission statement should pass the "stranger test": if someone who knows nothing about your organization reads it, do they immediately understand what you do and for whom? If not, keep refining.

Create Marketing Materials with Canva AI

Three materials every nonprofit needs — made in minutes.

Social Media Post

  • Search "Instagram post" or "Facebook post" in Canva
  • Use Magic Design: describe your nonprofit and the post's purpose
  • Apply your brand colors and logo
  • Create a template you can reuse weekly

One-Pager

  • Search "nonprofit one-pager" or "fact sheet"
  • Include: mission, key programs, impact data, contact info
  • This is what you hand to a potential donor or partner
  • Export as PDF for email and print

Event Flyer

  • Search "event flyer" or "fundraiser" template
  • Include: event name, date, location, purpose, RSVP info
  • Use Canva AI to generate copy if needed
  • Share digitally and print for community boards

Brand consistency tip: In Canva, create a Brand Kit (free tier allows one) — upload your logo, set your colors and fonts. Every new design will start with your brand elements.

Canva is your best friend for nonprofit marketing materials. Three essential pieces: a social media post template you can reuse, a one-pager that summarizes your organization for donors and partners, and an event flyer. For each one, start with a Canva template, use the Magic Design feature to get AI-generated options based on your description, then customize with your brand colors, fonts, and logo. The most important tip: set up a Brand Kit in Canva. Even the free tier allows one. Upload your logo, enter your brand colors and fonts, and every new design will automatically use your visual identity. This saves time and ensures consistency.

Worth Ch. 9

HR in Nonprofits: A Different World

Nonprofit HR operates under unique conditions that differ fundamentally from corporate settings.

Dimension Corporate Sector Nonprofit Sector
Motivation Salary, bonuses, equity Mission alignment, intrinsic rewards, public service
Compensation Market-rate or above Typically 15–30% below market; offset by benefits, flexibility, meaning
Team Size Specialized departments Small teams wearing many hats; ED as generalist
Workforce Mix All paid employees Paid staff + volunteers + interns + AmeriCorps + contractors
HR Infrastructure Dedicated HR department Often no HR staff; ED or office manager handles everything

Key insight: People join nonprofits for the mission. They leave because of burnout, low pay, and poor management. Your HR plan must address both retention and recruitment.

Worth Chapter 9 frames the nonprofit HR challenge. People don't work at nonprofits for the money — they come for the mission. But mission alone doesn't pay rent. The nonprofit sector has chronic compensation gaps, small teams where everyone wears multiple hats, and often no formal HR infrastructure at all. The executive director might be the recruiter, the HR manager, the volunteer coordinator, and the program director all in one. Your HR plan needs to be realistic about these constraints while still creating a professional, supportive work environment. The number one reason people leave nonprofits is burnout, not pay. Design systems that prevent it.
Dicke & Ott (2003)

Post-9/11 HR Challenges

How crisis events reshape nonprofit human resource management — and why it still matters today.

What Changed After 9/11

  • Background checks: Became standard practice, especially for organizations serving vulnerable populations
  • Liability awareness: Organizations faced new scrutiny around volunteer screening and supervision
  • Security protocols: Physical safety, data protection, emergency preparedness
  • Government contracting: Stricter compliance requirements for nonprofits receiving federal funds

Still Relevant Today

  • COVID-19 brought another wave of HR transformation: remote work, hybrid models, mental health support
  • Increased emphasis on duty of care — not just to clients but to staff and volunteers
  • HR compliance is now a funder expectation — grant applications ask about HR policies, background checks, and volunteer management

Every crisis raises the bar for nonprofit HR. Your HR plan should anticipate, not just react.

Dicke and Ott wrote this in 2003, but the pattern they identified repeats. After 9/11, nonprofits had to dramatically upgrade their HR practices — background checks became standard, liability insurance became essential, and government contracts came with new compliance strings attached. We saw the same thing after COVID: remote work policies, mental health accommodations, flexible scheduling. Each crisis raises the baseline expectation for how nonprofits manage their people. For your HR plan, don't just design for normal operations — think about what policies you need for crisis situations, for remote work, for protecting both your staff and your clients.
Handy, Mook & Quarter (2008)

Paid Staff vs. Volunteers

The interchangeability question: When can volunteers replace paid staff? When can't they?

Volunteers Can

  • Supplement capacity during events and campaigns
  • Bring specialized pro bono expertise (legal, accounting, IT)
  • Serve on boards and advisory committees
  • Provide community connection and credibility
  • Mentor, tutor, or provide peer support

Volunteers Cannot

  • Replace core staff performing daily operations
  • Be relied on for consistent, long-term availability
  • Handle sensitive HR, finance, or compliance functions
  • Be managed the same way as employees (no disciplinary authority)
  • Work under conditions that violate labor laws (DOL guidelines)

Ethical consideration: Using volunteers to avoid paying staff is exploitation, not efficiency. Your plan should clearly distinguish volunteer roles from paid positions.

Handy, Mook, and Quarter's research asks a question every nonprofit leader faces: when is it appropriate to use volunteers instead of paid staff? The answer has both practical and ethical dimensions. Practically, volunteers are unreliable for core operations — they have other commitments, they come and go, you can't discipline them the way you can employees. Ethically, substituting volunteers for paid staff to save money is a form of labor exploitation that undermines the sector. Your HR plan should have clear role boundaries: these are paid positions because they require consistency, expertise, and accountability; these are volunteer roles because they supplement our capacity and provide meaningful community engagement. The Department of Labor has clear guidelines about this distinction — violating them can result in serious legal consequences.
NML · Challenges for Nonprofit Managers

Building a Sustainable Workforce

The Big Challenges

1
Retention & Burnout: Average nonprofit turnover is 19%. Emotional labor, understaffing, and low pay drive people out.
2
Compensation Gaps: Nonprofits compete with government and private sector for talent but can't match salaries. Must compete on mission, culture, and flexibility.
3
DEI in Hiring: The sector is predominantly white in leadership despite serving diverse communities. Intentional recruitment, equitable pay, and inclusive culture are essential.
4
Remote & Hybrid Work: Post-COVID, staff expect flexibility. How do you maintain culture and accountability in distributed teams?

Strategies That Work

  • Invest in professional development — training budgets, conference attendance, tuition support
  • Create clear advancement paths — even in small organizations, define growth opportunities
  • Prioritize work-life balance — flexible schedules, mental health days, reasonable workloads
  • Pay as well as you can — research market rates, be transparent about constraints, offer non-monetary benefits
  • Build inclusive culture — DEI is not a checkbox; it's how decisions are made daily
The Nonprofit Management and Leadership article on workforce challenges paints a sobering picture. Nearly one in five nonprofit employees turns over each year. The causes are well-documented: burnout from doing too much with too little, compensation that doesn't keep pace with the cost of living, and organizational cultures that sometimes prioritize mission over people. The DEI challenge is particularly important — nonprofit leadership remains disproportionately white even though many organizations serve communities of color. Your HR plan needs to address these issues head-on. What's your retention strategy? How will you approach equitable compensation? What does your DEI commitment look like in practice, not just on paper? These are the questions that separate a thoughtful plan from a generic one.

AI Demo

Create an Org Chart with Canva

Step-by-step: building a professional organizational chart for your nonprofit.

1
Go to Canva.com — free account is sufficient. Search templates for "organizational chart" or "org chart."
2
Choose a clean template — pick a simple, professional design. Avoid overly colorful or corporate templates. You want it to look nonprofit-appropriate.
3
Customize the structure — Board of Directors at top, Executive Director below, then program staff, admin staff, and volunteer coordinator. Add your specific positions.
4
Use Canva AI (Magic Write) — ask it to suggest position titles based on your nonprofit's mission area. Review and adjust.
5
Export as PNG or PDF — include in your HR plan document and on your website's Team page.
Let me walk you through creating an org chart in Canva. This is a practical skill you'll use repeatedly. Open Canva, search for org chart templates, and pick something clean and professional. Your structure should show: Board of Directors at the top, reporting down to the Executive Director, then your program staff and administrative staff. Add a volunteer coordinator position if volunteers are a significant part of your model. Canva's AI features can help suggest position titles, but you need to customize them for your specific organization. Export the final chart as a PNG for your website and as part of your HR plan submission.
AI Demo

Draft Job Descriptions with AI

Use Claude to draft professional nonprofit job descriptions, then refine.

Prompt:

"Draft a job description for an Executive Director of a small nonprofit called [Your Org Name] focused on [mission area] in [location]. Include: job title, reports to (Board of Directors), position summary, key responsibilities (6–8), qualifications (required and preferred), compensation range, and benefits. The organization has a budget of approximately [$X] and a team of [N] staff plus volunteers."

Salary research: Use GuideStar/Candid compensation reports, Bureau of Labor Statistics, and Idealist.org salary data to validate the AI's compensation suggestions. Florida-specific: check Indeed and LinkedIn for Central Florida nonprofit salary ranges.

Here's the second AI demo. Job descriptions are another area where AI is genuinely useful as a starting point. The prompt includes the key details Claude needs: organization name, mission, location, budget size, and team size. The output will give you a solid first draft, but you need to customize it. Most importantly, validate the salary range — AI doesn't know current local market rates. Use GuideStar's compensation reports, the Bureau of Labor Statistics, Indeed, and LinkedIn salary data for Central Florida nonprofits. Your job descriptions should be realistic — don't list an ED salary of $120,000 if your budget is $200,000. That's not credible.

Two website additions this week. First, a Team page that shows your organizational structure — embed the org chart from Canva, list key positions, and briefly describe what each role does. This tells funders and partners that you've thought carefully about capacity. Second, a Get Involved or Volunteer page. This is your public-facing volunteer recruitment tool. List specific volunteer opportunities, what skills you're looking for, expected time commitments, and how to sign up. Even if your nonprofit is hypothetical, this page demonstrates that you understand community engagement. By the end of this week, you should have five core pages on your site.

  • It is iterative: Plans change as environments change — the process matters as much as the product
  • It is participatory: Board, staff, constituents, and stakeholders should all have input

Worth Ch. 7, pg. 181–198

Worth's Chapter 7 gives us the conceptual framework for strategic planning. I want to emphasize something that many people miss: strategic planning is a process, not a product. Yes, you produce a document at the end. But the real value is in the thinking that goes into it — the honest assessment of your environment, the difficult choices about priorities, the alignment of resources with goals. The process I've outlined here — vision, environmental scan, goals, strategies, implementation, evaluation — is cyclical. You don't do it once and put it on a shelf. Effective organizations revisit their strategic plan regularly, adjusting as conditions change. For your assignment, you're producing a document. But I want you to engage with the process — think deeply about the choices you're making and why.
Research

Research on Nonprofit Strategic Management

Stone, Bigelow & Crittenden (1999) synthesized decades of strategic management research in the nonprofit sector. Their key findings challenge simplistic approaches.

How Nonprofits Differ

  • Multiple stakeholders with competing interests — donors want efficiency, staff want autonomy, constituents want responsiveness
  • Mission constraints limit strategic options — you can't just pivot to whatever's profitable
  • Resource dependency shapes strategy — whoever funds you influences your direction
  • Ambiguous performance metrics — "success" is harder to define than profit

What the Research Shows

  • Formal strategic planning does improve organizational performance — but only if implementation follows
  • Board engagement in strategy is critical — boards that only do oversight underperform
  • External environment matters more than internal factors for many nonprofits
  • Strategy formulation and strategy implementation are different competencies
Stone, Bigelow, and Crittenden's synthesis is foundational. They reviewed the entire body of research on nonprofit strategic management and found some important patterns. First, you cannot simply apply business strategy frameworks to nonprofits without modification. Nonprofits operate with multiple stakeholders who often want different things. Your donors might prioritize low overhead, while your staff prioritize competitive salaries, and your constituents prioritize program quality. Strategy requires balancing these tensions, not resolving them. Second, the research shows that formal planning does improve outcomes — but only when organizations actually implement their plans. The number of nonprofits with beautiful strategic plans gathering dust on a shelf is staggering. Third, and this is crucial for your assignment: strategy formulation and strategy implementation are different skills. Knowing what to do and knowing how to do it are not the same thing. Your strategic plan needs both.

Analysis

SWOT Analysis Deep Dive

For your nonprofit, be honest about where you're starting from. Every organization has weaknesses and threats — acknowledging them is a sign of maturity, not failure.

Strengths (Internal)

  • Founder expertise and passion
  • Clear, compelling mission
  • Engaged board members
  • Strong community relationships

Weaknesses (Internal)

  • No track record or brand recognition
  • Limited funding and staff
  • Unproven programs
  • Heavy reliance on founder

Opportunities (External)

  • Growing awareness of issue area
  • New funding streams available
  • Potential community partnerships
  • Policy changes that support mission

Threats (External)

  • Established competitors
  • Economic uncertainty
  • Donor fatigue in your issue area
  • Regulatory barriers

These are examples. Your SWOT should reflect your specific nonprofit's context.

The SWOT analysis is the analytical engine of your strategic plan. Strengths and weaknesses are internal — things about your organization that you can control. Opportunities and threats are external — forces in your environment that you can respond to but not control. The examples I'm showing are common for startup nonprofits, but yours should be specific to your situation. Be honest. If your weakness is that you have no track record and no funding, say so. If the threat is that three established organizations already serve your target population, acknowledge it. The strategic plan is more credible when it demonstrates awareness of challenges, not just aspirations. A plan that only lists strengths and opportunities is a marketing brochure, not a strategic document.
Application

From SWOT to Strategy

The power of SWOT is in the cross-analysis — matching internal factors with external factors to generate strategies.

CombinationStrategic ApproachExample
Strength + Opportunity Leverage — use strengths to capitalize on opportunities Strong community ties + growing awareness = grassroots fundraising campaign
Weakness + Opportunity Improve — address weaknesses to take advantage of opportunities No track record + new grant program = pilot project to build evidence
Strength + Threat Defend — use strengths to mitigate threats Compelling mission + donor fatigue = differentiated storytelling strategy
Weakness + Threat Avoid — minimize risk exposure Limited funding + economic downturn = conservative Year 1 budget with reserves

Your task: For each strategic goal in your plan, show how it connects to specific SWOT findings. This demonstrates analytical rigor, not just aspiration.

This is where SWOT becomes genuinely useful. Many people do SWOT analysis as a brainstorming exercise and then ignore it when writing their goals. That's backwards. The whole point is to generate strategies from the intersection of internal and external factors. The four combinations give you four strategic approaches. Strength plus opportunity is your leverage zone — where you can be most aggressive. Weakness plus threat is your danger zone — where you need to be most cautious. The example I use often: if your threat is limited funding and your strength is strong community ties, the strategic response is a grassroots fundraising campaign that leverages your community relationships. That's a strategy that directly addresses a threat using an existing strength. In your strategic plan, I want to see this kind of analytical connection. Don't just list goals — show me how each goal responds to your SWOT findings.

AI Demo

SWOT Brainstorming with AI

Use AI to generate SWOT items — then critically evaluate and filter.

ChatGPT Prompt I'm developing a strategic plan for a new nonprofit focused on [your issue area] in [your city/region]. Generate a SWOT analysis with 5 items for each category (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats). Consider the current funding environment, policy landscape, community needs, and competitive landscape for nonprofits in this space.

Critical evaluation checklist:

  • Is it specific to your context? Generic items like "passionate leadership" don't add value
  • Is it evidence-based? Can you cite a source for external factors?
  • Is it honest? AI tends to be optimistic — add the hard truths it misses
  • Is it actionable? If a SWOT item doesn't suggest a strategic response, reconsider it

Process: Use AI output as a brainstorming starting point. Keep the items that are specific and verifiable. Replace the generic items with your own research. Document everything in your AI Process Log.

AI is genuinely useful for SWOT brainstorming because it can quickly surface factors you might not have considered. But — and this is a big but — AI tends to produce generic SWOT items. "Passionate leadership" as a strength, "limited funding" as a weakness — these appear in literally every nonprofit SWOT analysis ever written. They're not wrong, but they're not useful. What I'm looking for in your strategic plan is specificity. Not "growing awareness of the issue" as an opportunity, but "the 2026 Florida legislative session includes three proposed bills related to our issue area, creating potential advocacy partnerships and state funding." Not "established competitors" as a threat, but "XYZ Organization has operated in our target community for 15 years with a $2 million annual budget and strong name recognition." Use AI to get started, then make every item specific, evidence-based, and actionable.
AI Demo

Draft Strategic Goal Narratives

AI can help you articulate strategic goals — but the strategic thinking must be yours.

Claude Prompt Write a strategic goal narrative for a nonprofit strategic plan. The goal is: [your goal]. Context: [brief description of your nonprofit and its stage of development]. Include: (1) goal statement, (2) rationale connecting to mission, (3) 3 specific strategies to achieve this goal, (4) measurable milestones for Year 1. Write in a professional, concise tone appropriate for a strategic planning document.

Revision process:

  • Check alignment: Does the goal connect to your SWOT findings?
  • Check feasibility: Can you realistically accomplish this in your Year 1 timeline with your budget?
  • Check measurability: Will you know if you've succeeded? What's the indicator?
  • Revise for voice: The final document should sound like you, not like AI
For the strategic goal narratives, AI can help you with structure and language, but the substance has to come from you. Use this prompt to generate a first draft of each goal narrative, then revise extensively. The revision process is where the learning happens. Check each goal against three criteria: alignment with your SWOT, feasibility given your resources, and measurability. If you can't measure progress toward a goal, it's an aspiration, not a strategy. And please — revise for your own voice. I've been reading your work all semester. If your strategic plan suddenly reads like a corporate consulting report, I'll notice. Use AI as a drafting assistant, but make the final product yours.

Your website update this week has two parts. First, add an "Our Strategy" or "Impact" page that communicates your strategic direction in public-facing language.

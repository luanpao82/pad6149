Foundations

What Is the Nonprofit Sector?

The Three-Sector Model

  • Government — public goods, regulation, redistribution
  • Business — private goods, profit-driven, market exchange
  • Nonprofit — mission-driven, voluntary, tax-exempt

Key Characteristics

  • Organized and institutional
  • Private (separate from government)
  • Non-profit-distributing
  • Self-governing
  • Voluntary in nature
  • Governed by a board of directors
Worth (2020), Ch. 1 — "An Overview of the Nonprofit Sector"
Worth lays out the structural-operational definition of nonprofit organizations. The nondistribution constraint is the legal cornerstone — nonprofits can generate surplus revenue, but they cannot distribute it to owners or shareholders. This is what fundamentally distinguishes a nonprofit from a for-profit business. Also emphasize that "nonprofit" does not mean "no money" — it means no private inurement. These organizations need revenue to survive. The five characteristics listed here come from the Johns Hopkins Comparative Nonprofit Sector Project.
Scale

Size & Scope of the Sector

~1.8M Registered nonprofits in the U.S.
$2.62T Annual revenue
12.5M Paid employees (3rd largest workforce)
  • Nonprofits account for approximately 5.7% of GDP
  • Sector has grown faster than both business and government over the past 25 years
  • Over 60 million Americans volunteer annually (~$195 billion in estimated value)
  • Roughly 1 in 10 U.S. workers is employed by a nonprofit
Sherlock & Gravelle (2009); Worth (2020), Ch. 2
These numbers matter because they establish that the nonprofit sector is not a marginal part of the economy — it is massive. When students say they want to "start a nonprofit," they are entering a sector with substantial infrastructure, regulation, and competition. The Sherlock and Gravelle report provides the tax policy context: the sector's growth has prompted ongoing debates about whether tax exemption is still justified across all types of nonprofits. Note that these figures include hospitals and universities, which account for a disproportionate share of revenue.
Legal Structure

Types of Nonprofit Organizations

501(c)(3) — Tax-Exempt, Tax-Deductible

  • Public charities — broad public support (e.g., food banks, schools, hospitals)
  • Private foundations — typically single-source funding (e.g., Gates Foundation)
  • Donations are tax-deductible for donors
  • Limited lobbying; no partisan political activity

Other 501(c) Types

  • 501(c)(4) — Social welfare orgs (e.g., advocacy groups)
  • 501(c)(6) — Business leagues, chambers of commerce
  • 501(c)(7) — Social and recreational clubs
  • 29 total categories under Section 501(c)

For your nonprofit: What NTEE code best describes your organization? What 501(c) classification would you seek? Most of you will be 501(c)(3) public charities — but know why.

The NTEE (National Taxonomy of Exempt Entities) is the classification system used by the IRS and Candid/GuideStar to categorize nonprofits. Students should look up the NTEE codes and identify where their proposed nonprofit fits. This matters for benchmarking — when they later research comparable organizations, they will use NTEE codes to find peers. Emphasize the public charity vs. private foundation distinction: the public support test determines classification, and it affects governance, grantmaking, and donor relations.
Theory

Why Do Nonprofits Exist?

  • Market failure theory (Weisbrod) — markets underproduce public goods; nonprofits fill the gap
  • Government failure theory (Weisbrod) — government serves the median voter; nonprofits serve heterogeneous demand
  • Contract failure theory (Hansmann) — when consumers cannot easily evaluate quality, the nondistribution constraint signals trustworthiness
  • Voluntary failure theory (Salamon) — nonprofits are the default; government steps in when voluntary action fails

Chang & Tuckman (1996): Nonprofits produce public goods, quasi-public goods, and private goods with positive externalities. The mix matters — and it shapes how you fund your work.

Chang & Tuckman (1996); Worth (2020), Ch. 3
This is the most conceptually dense slide of the week. The key insight for students: there is no single theory that explains why nonprofits exist. Each theory highlights a different rationale, and most real-world nonprofits draw on multiple justifications simultaneously. Chang and Tuckman's contribution is practical — by classifying the types of goods nonprofits produce, they help us understand revenue strategy. If your nonprofit primarily produces public goods, you will rely on donations and grants. If you produce private goods with positive externalities, you can charge fees. This connects directly to their business model decisions later in the course.
History

Nonprofits in American History

Colonial Mutual aid societies, churches, and charitable trusts — community self-governance before the state
1800s Tocqueville's "voluntary associations" — hospitals, universities, and reform movements emerge
Progressive Professionalization of charity — social work, foundations (Carnegie, Rockefeller), and the Community Chest
1950s–70s Great Society expansion — government contracts with nonprofits to deliver social services at scale
1980s–00s Reagan-era devolution, rise of "nonprofit management" as a field, accountability and outcomes focus
2010s–Now Social enterprise, collective impact, racial equity reckoning, tech-enabled philanthropy

Key point: The sector is always evolving. Your nonprofit enters a living tradition, not a static system.

Hammack (2002) — "Nonprofits in American History"
Hammack's essay is essential for understanding that the nonprofit sector is not a natural or inevitable feature of American life — it was constructed over centuries through legal decisions, cultural norms, and political choices. The key turning points: the Dartmouth College case (1819) establishing the legal independence of charitable corporations; the creation of the income tax and charitable deduction (1913/1917); and the massive expansion of government-nonprofit contracting in the 1960s–70s. Students should understand that their nonprofit will be shaped by this history whether they know it or not.
Rethinking Assumptions

The Pollyanna Principles

Gottlieb (2009) challenges conventional nonprofit thinking:

  • Create the future, not just react to the past
  • The end you are working toward must be visionary, not incremental
  • Everyone in a healthy community is a contributor, not just a recipient
  • "Ordinary people" are capable of extraordinary impact
  • Nonprofits must be guided by what creates the most good, not what raises the most money

For your nonprofit: Is your mission statement about fixing a problem or creating a future? There is a difference — and it shapes everything from your programs to your fundraising pitch.

Gottlieb (2009), pp. 1–117 — The Pollyanna Principles
Gottlieb's work is the counterweight to the more technical readings this week. She argues that most nonprofits are trapped in a deficit-based mindset — defining themselves by the problems they fight rather than the futures they are building. This is a powerful reframe for students designing new organizations. Push them to ask: Does my mission statement describe a world I want to create, or just a problem I want to reduce? The practical implication is real — asset-based framing attracts different funders, different board members, and different community partners than deficit-based framing.
Application

Connecting to Your Nonprofit

Pull out your concept statement from Week 1 and think through:

  • Sector placement: Where does your org fit in the three-sector model?
  • Legal structure: Will you be a 501(c)(3) public charity? Why?
  • NTEE classification: What code best describes your work?
  • Theoretical justification: Which failure theory best explains why your nonprofit needs to exist?
  • Ecosystem: Who else is already doing this work? Are they competitors, collaborators, or both?

This week's workbook: Research 2–3 existing organizations in your space. Identify your role-model org — the one you most want to learn from.

This is the bridge slide. Everything conceptual from the first half of the deck now gets applied to their specific nonprofit. Give students 5–10 minutes to work through these questions independently or in breakout rooms. The ecosystem question is critical — many students assume they are the first to think of their idea. The workbook task forces them to discover who is already in the space and learn from them. Emphasize: finding existing organizations doing similar work is a good thing, not a threat. It validates demand.

Assignment: Interview Plan

Submit your interview plan for the nonprofit leader interview:

  • Who will you contact? (Name, title, organization)
  • Why this person or organization? (Connection to your nonprofit concept)
  • 8–10 interview questions covering:
    • Origin story and mission
    • Funding model and financial sustainability
    • Governance and leadership structure
    • Biggest challenges and lessons learned
    • Advice for someone starting a new nonprofit

Tip: Choose someone whose organization resembles what you want to build. The closer the fit, the more useful the conversation.

Case: Rowe & Conway Dato-On (2013), Chapter 1
The interview plan is a low-stakes assignment designed to get students moving on their primary research early. The case reading from Rowe and Conway Dato-On provides a model for how social entrepreneurs research and learn from existing organizations. Common student mistakes: choosing someone too famous or too distant to actually interview; writing vague questions; not explaining why this particular person matters. Push them to be specific and strategic. The outreach email they draft in the workbook task is the practical companion to this assignment.
AI Demo

Researching Your Role-Model Org

Use Perplexity.ai to research a nonprofit you admire:

Tell me about [Organization Name]: their mission, annual budget, key programs, leadership team, major funding sources, and any recent news or challenges.

Then verify:

  • Check GuideStar/Candid (candid.org) for Form 990 data — actual revenue, expenses, executive compensation
  • Visit the organization's website for current programs and leadership
  • Search ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer for financial trends

Rule: AI is a starting point, not an endpoint. Always verify financial data and leadership names against primary sources.

Walk through a live demo if possible. Pick a well-known nonprofit — say, Feeding America or Habitat for Humanity — and run the Perplexity query. Then open Candid and show students how to find the 990 data. The point is not that AI is unreliable — it is actually quite useful for this kind of research — but that financial figures and personnel details must be verified. This is also a good moment to introduce students to Form 990 as a public document. Many students do not know that nonprofit financial data is publicly available.
AI Demo

Drafting Your Outreach Email

Use Claude.ai to draft a professional interview request:

Help me write a professional email to [Name], [Title] at [Organization], requesting a 30-minute informational interview. I'm a graduate student in UCF's Master of Nonprofit Management program building a nonprofit focused on [your topic]. I want to learn about their experience with [specific aspect].

After you get the draft:

  • Personalize it — reference something specific about their work
  • Be concrete — propose specific dates and a time limit (30 min)
  • Be professional — use your UCF email address
  • Follow up — if no response in 5–7 days, send one polite follow-up
This is a practical skill-building moment. Many graduate students have never written a cold outreach email to a professional, and they are often anxious about it. Show the Claude output, then model how to revise it: add a specific reference to the person's work, adjust the tone to sound like a real human, and cut any generic flattery. Emphasize that AI-generated emails sound AI-generated if you do not edit them. The goal is to use AI as a drafting tool, not a replacement for your own voice. Also remind students: most nonprofit professionals are generous with their time when a graduate student asks respectfully.

Redesigning the MNM Capstone

PAD 6149 — Nonprofit Administration: Build Your Nonprofit with AI

Dr. Seongho An
School of Public Administration
University of Central Florida

Thank you for the time today. I want to walk you through a ground-up redesign of PAD 6149, our MNM capstone. The redesign takes effect in Fall 2026. My goal in the next fifteen minutes is simple: explain what has changed, why, and what students will walk out of this course with that they did not before. I'll save questions for the end.

Starting Point

The Capstone We Inherited

Under its current design, PAD 6149 was organized around three retrospective activities:

  • Discussion Board Participation — 50%. Students posted ~1,000 words per week reacting to readings.
  • ePortfolio Assignment — 30%. Students uploaded prior MNM coursework with short reflection papers organized by NASPAA competency.
  • Leadership Case Study — 20%. A single written case analysis.

AI tools were explicitly prohibited: "The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools… is not permitted in this class."

This is where we were. The course was effectively a review semester: students looked backward at work they had already done, reflected on it, and posted in discussion boards. That design had integrity when it was built — the ePortfolio was the intended synthesis artifact. But it leaves students with no new, forward-facing deliverable. And the explicit AI prohibition, written in 2024, is now out of step with what every practicing nonprofit leader actually does on the job.

The Redesign

One Sentence

Students do not write about nonprofit management. They build a nonprofit. — Core Design Principle, PAD 6149 (Fall 2026)

Every weekly deliverable in the redesigned course adds a real component to a single cohesive organization — governance documents, budget, staffing plan, strategic plan, program design, fundraising strategy, advocacy plan, and a public-facing website. By Week 14, the student has produced a complete nonprofit blueprint.

If you take only one idea from this briefing, let it be this one. The capstone is no longer a retrospective. It is a design studio. Students spend fifteen weeks building one nonprofit organization from scratch — a real one, documented publicly. Every textbook concept, every prior assignment they've written for other MNM courses, gets reactivated in service of that one organization. It's the difference between writing a memoir and producing a working prototype.

Side-by-Side

What Changed

Current

  • 3 assessments (discussion boards, ePortfolio, one case study)
  • Retrospective synthesis of prior coursework
  • 50% of grade = weekly discussion posts
  • AI use prohibited
  • No public deliverable
  • No practitioner engagement

Fall 2026

  • 15 weekly deliverables, all feeding one organization
  • Prior coursework reactivated into a new, student-owned org
  • No discussion boards; no standalone ePortfolio
  • AI use required, with ethical framework
  • Public website + YouTube pitch video
  • Service learning: practitioner mentor interview

Every dimension of the course changed. The assessment model shifted from three large bundled items to fifteen linked weekly deliverables, each one small enough to be submitted well but additive enough that by the end of the term, the student has something substantial. We removed the discussion boards, which accounted for half the prior grade, because the signal-to-noise ratio on asynchronous 1,000-word posts was poor. We replaced the retrospective ePortfolio with a forward-facing public website. And we reversed the AI policy — I'll explain the rationale in a moment.

Design Philosophy

Two Ideas Driving the Redesign

1. Execution is the capstone competency

Graduate students in a professional program don't need more exposure to concepts. They need repeated practice producing the artifacts a nonprofit leader actually produces — bylaws, budgets, logic models, pitch decks.

2. AI fluency is a professional competency

Nonprofit managers already use AI for grant writing, donor communications, program evaluation, and strategic planning. Prohibiting it leaves graduates unprepared. Requiring it — with a documented ethical framework — prepares them.

Both ideas point in the same direction: a capstone measured by what students can do, not what they can recall.

The redesign rests on two claims. The first is pedagogical: at the end of a professional master's degree, the right evidence of mastery is a produced artifact, not a recalled fact. The second is environmental: our graduates enter a sector that already uses AI daily, so we need to teach them to use it responsibly rather than pretend it doesn't exist. These two ideas are complementary — building a real nonprofit is the natural context in which to learn applied AI use, because both require judgment, iteration, and critique rather than memorization.

The semester follows the arc a real nonprofit founder would follow. We start with concept and mentor-practitioner research in weeks one and two. Weeks three and four establish the structural spine — the website and governance documents. Weeks five and six address people and identity. Weeks seven and eight cover money. Weeks nine and ten move into strategy and evaluation. The closing stretch covers leadership, AI-enabled communications, advocacy, and the final pitch. At no point is a student starting from a blank page; each week builds on the previous one.

The Artifact

One Integrating Deliverable: A Live Website

From Week 3 forward, every major assignment produces two outputs: a document submitted to Webcourses, and a corresponding page on the student's public nonprofit website.

Platform choice is the student's — Wix, Squarespace, WordPress, Google Sites, or a code-based option. The website is graded on content and professionalism, not technology.

At the end of the term, it is a public artifact the student can show a future employer or funder.

The website is what holds the course together. Every document a student submits has a corresponding public-facing summary on their nonprofit's website. We're not asking students to learn web development — they can use any no-code tool. What we're asking is that they treat their work as something a stranger will see, which raises the quality bar naturally. And because the website is public, it doubles as a portfolio piece. A student going into a job interview can point a hiring manager to a live URL showing a complete organizational blueprint they designed.

Service Learning

Mentor Interview: Grounding the Semester in Practice

What students do

  • Week 2 — Interview Plan. Identify a practicing nonprofit leader in their focus area; draft 8–10 research questions.
  • Week 4 — Mentor Interview Report (8 pts). A 4–5 page report synthesizing the interview and applying insights to their own nonprofit design.

Why it matters

  • Grounds abstract course concepts in practitioner reality
  • Builds a professional network before graduation
  • Reconnects MNM students to the Central Florida nonprofit ecosystem

This is the only new component not adapted from prior MNM coursework. It is service learning in the formal sense — applied engagement with the community the program serves.

The mentor interview is the one piece of the redesigned course that isn't pulled from prior MNM coursework — and it's deliberately the piece that puts students in contact with practitioners. Every student identifies a working nonprofit leader, prepares a structured interview, conducts it, and writes up what they learned. This does three things. It grounds the semester in real practice rather than abstraction. It gives students a networking artifact they can cite in job interviews. And it quietly rebuilds the program's visible presence in the Central Florida nonprofit community, because each semester adds a new cohort of students reaching out to local leaders.

AI Policy

AI as Required Professional Competency

Current Policy

"The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools, via website, app, or any other access, is not permitted in this class."

New Policy (2026)

AI use is required. Students document their AI use on every major deliverable and are graded on the quality of their judgment, not the volume of their output.

The Four Rules

  • Authorship. Students must author, direct, critically evaluate, and own all AI-assisted work.
  • No raw output. Submitting unrevised AI text is treated as academic dishonesty.
  • Disclosure. AI-generated images that could be mistaken for photographs must be labeled.
  • Privacy. No real donor, client, or personnel data in AI tools — fictional only.

All required tools have free tiers. No paid subscriptions.

This is the policy change I expect most discussion on. The rationale: practicing nonprofit leaders are already using AI for grant drafting, donor communications, program evaluation, and social media. A graduate who cannot use these tools ethically and critically is already behind. Prohibiting AI in a capstone course in 2026 would be the equivalent of prohibiting spreadsheets in a finance course in 2005. The policy has guardrails — authorship, disclosure, privacy, and an ethical framework that treats AI as a tool the student directs, not an author that replaces them. We use only free-tier tools, so there is no financial barrier for students.

Accreditation

NASPAA Competency Alignment

Every major capstone deliverable maps to one of the five NASPAA universal competencies. Each rubric names the competency being assessed.

  • C1 Lead and manage in a mission-driven sector — Strategic Plan, Leadership Case Study, Final Pitch
  • C2 Analyze, synthesize, think critically — Program Evaluation Framework
  • C3 Communicate with a diverse workforce and citizenry — HR & Volunteer Management Plan
  • C4 Articulate a public service perspective — Board Governance Policy
  • C5 Participate in the policy process — Policy Advocacy Plan

The redesign preserves the competency coverage of the ePortfolio but moves the evidence from reflection on past work to demonstration in new work.

Because PAD 6149 is the capstone in a NASPAA-accredited program, the redesign had to preserve competency coverage. It does. Every one of the five universal competencies has a specific anchor deliverable in the new course — and in every case, the evidence is stronger than it was under the ePortfolio model. The old model asked students to reflect on past competency. The new model asks them to demonstrate it, freshly, on an organization they designed. For accreditation review, the artifacts produced in this course are far more defensible.

Outcomes

What Students Take With Them

A complete organizational blueprint

Mission, governance, staffing, budget, strategy, programs, evaluation, fundraising, advocacy — for an organization of their own design.

A public nonprofit website

Live, shareable, and linked from a résumé. Functions as a portfolio piece for employers and funders.

A final pitch video

A YouTube-hosted pitch presenting the organization to potential supporters — usable as a writing sample or interview artifact.

Documented AI fluency

Demonstrated, critical use of AI tools across fourteen weeks of professional deliverables — a growing sector requirement.

A practitioner network connection

A completed mentor interview and relationship with a working nonprofit leader in their focus area.

Evidence of NASPAA mastery

Fresh, rubric-aligned demonstration across all five competencies, not retrospective reflection on prior work.

This is the "so what" slide. The old capstone produced a reflection paper and a collection of prior assignments. The new capstone produces six distinct, durable assets — a blueprint, a live website, a pitch video, documented AI fluency, a practitioner connection, and fresh competency evidence. In some cases, students will actually launch the organization they designed. Even when they don't, every one of these assets is portable into a job application, a funder conversation, or a next-degree application.

Program Value

Why This Matters for the MNM Program

Differentiation

Most MNM and MPA capstones are reflective. A design-studio capstone where students build and publish a real nonprofit is a distinctive recruitment and reputation asset.

Recruitment

Every cohort produces visible alumni work — live websites and pitch videos — that can be showcased to prospective students and during accreditation review.

Community presence

The mentor interview component re-engages MNM students with Central Florida nonprofit leaders every semester.

Accreditation readiness

NASPAA competency evidence is demonstrated on new, original work rather than reflected upon retrospectively — stronger artifacts for site visits.

Zooming out from the individual student: why is this worth the program's time and the board's attention. Four reasons. One, it differentiates us — few MNM programs in the country structure their capstone as a design studio, and the ones that do tend to be elite private programs. Two, it gives us visible outputs every semester we can point prospective students to. Three, it quietly reinforces our community presence through the mentor interview. And four, for NASPAA site visits, fresh original artifacts are stronger evidence than reflection papers on work produced in other classes.

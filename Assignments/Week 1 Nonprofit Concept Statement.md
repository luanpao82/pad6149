## What Is This Assignment?

Welcome to your final semester. This course is structured differently from anything else in the MNM program — instead of writing about nonprofits, you'll be **designing one from the ground up**.

This assignment is the starting point: a one-page Concept Statement that defines the organization you'll develop throughout the semester. What community need would you address? What population would you serve? What would the organization look like?

The concept doesn't need to be fully formed yet. It's a first draft — a working hypothesis that you'll refine as the semester progresses.

> [!info] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando
> Throughout the semester, all assignment examples will follow **one consistent organization** — **Asian Bridge Orlando**, a nonprofit serving Asian American and immigrant communities in the Orlando metro area. Each boxed example demonstrates what a submission for this organization might look like. Use it as a reference point as you develop your own.

This Concept Statement anchors every deliverable for the rest of the semester:

## What Do I Submit?

A **1-page, double-spaced** document covering these six elements:

### 1. Organization Name

Choose a working name for your nonprofit. It can change later — the point is to give the organization an identity from day one.

Characteristics of effective nonprofit names:
- Reflects the mission (e.g., Feeding America, Habitat for Humanity, Code.org)
- Memorable and easy to communicate
- Works well in professional contexts

> [!tip] AI Tip
> Try asking ChatGPT or Claude:
> ```
> I'm creating a nonprofit focused on [your issue] serving [your population]
> in [your location]. Suggest 10 creative organization names that are
> memorable, mission-driven, and professional.
> ```
> Use the output as a starting point — combine ideas or let it spark something original.

### 2. Mission Idea

Draft one or two sentences describing what your organization does. This is a working version — you'll polish it in Week 6.

A useful formula:

> **[Organization Name]** [what you do] **for** [who you serve] **to** [what change you create].

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Mission
> "Asian Bridge Orlando provides culturally responsive social services, language access programs, and community navigation support for Asian American and immigrant families in Central Florida to reduce isolation and strengthen civic participation."
>
> Notice the structure: **who** (Asian American and immigrant families in Central Florida), **what** (social services, language access, community navigation), and **why** (reduce isolation, strengthen civic participation) — all in one sentence.

### 3. Target Population

Define who your organization serves. Specificity matters — it shapes everything from program design to fundraising strategy.

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Target Population
> - ❌ "Immigrants" — too broad to be actionable
> - ✅ "First-generation Asian American and immigrant families in the Orlando metro area, particularly those with limited English proficiency who face barriers to accessing healthcare, legal services, and public benefits."

Consider:
- Geographic scope (neighborhood, city, state, national)
- Demographic characteristics
- The specific problem or gap this population faces

Use data to support your case. **Census Reporter** (censusreporter.org) lets you look up population demographics, income levels, and languages spoken at home for any area. **Social Explorer** (available free through the UCF Library databases) can visualize poverty rates, health disparities, and education levels by zip code. Even one or two data points can transform a vague concept into a compelling one.

### 4. Field / Issue Area

Identify the social issue your organization addresses. For reference:

| Field | Example Issues |
|---|---|
| Education | Early childhood, STEM access, adult literacy, college readiness |
| Health | Mental health access, substance abuse, chronic disease, maternal health |
| Housing | Homelessness prevention, affordable housing, domestic violence shelters |
| Environment | Urban green space, food security, climate justice, conservation |
| Arts & Culture | Arts education in underserved areas, cultural preservation |
| Immigration | Legal aid, language education, citizenship support, refugee resettlement |
| Youth Development | Mentoring, after-school programs, workforce development |
| Animal Welfare | Adoption programs, wildlife conservation, abuse prevention |
| Advocacy & Policy | Civic engagement, voting rights, criminal justice reform |

Scope matters — an organization that tries to do everything typically does nothing well. A quick search on **Google Scholar** (scholar.google.com) for recent studies on your issue area can help you identify where the real gaps are — and give you academic evidence to back up your concept.

### 5. 501(c)(3) Status Justification

Briefly explain why your organization qualifies for 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status. One or two sentences — this is a preliminary justification, not an IRS filing.

501(c)(3) qualifying purposes:
- **Charitable** — relieving poverty, advancing education, or lessening the burdens of government
- **Educational** — public education, training, research
- **Scientific** — scientific research for public benefit
- **Religious** — religious purposes
- **Literary** — literary or cultural purposes

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — 501(c)(3) Justification
> "Asian Bridge Orlando qualifies under the **charitable** and **educational** purposes of Section 501(c)(3) because we provide free social services, language access programs, and civic education to low-income immigrant families who face systemic barriers to community integration."

### 6. NTEE Code

The National Taxonomy of Exempt Entities (NTEE) is the classification system used by the IRS and **GuideStar / Candid** (candid.org) to categorize nonprofits by activity area. Search Candid by NTEE code and location to see who's already working in your space — their budgets, programs, and geographic reach. This is essential research: you need to know what exists before you can argue why your organization is needed.

> [!tip] AI Tip
> Try asking Perplexity.ai:
> ```
> What NTEE code would apply to a nonprofit that provides
> [describe your programs] for [your target population]?
> Give me the code, its description, and the major group it belongs to.
> ```

Major NTEE categories for reference:

| Code | Category |
|---|---|
| **A** | Arts, Culture, Humanities |
| **B** | Education |
| **E** | Health |
| **I** | Crime & Legal-Related |
| **J** | Employment |
| **K** | Food, Agriculture, Nutrition |
| **L** | Housing & Shelter |
| **O** | Youth Development |
| **P** | Human Services |
| **S** | Community Improvement |
| **W** | Public & Societal Benefit |

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — NTEE Code
> "NTEE Code: **P84** (Ethnic/Immigrant Services) — our organization provides culturally responsive social services and community integration support for Asian American and immigrant populations."

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Am I expected to actually launch this organization?**
A: No — but it happens more often than you'd think. Several MNM alumni have turned their capstone projects into real organizations. Approach it as if it's real, and the work will be stronger for it.

**Q: Can I change my concept later in the semester?**
A: The core focus area should remain consistent since every assignment builds on it. Details — name, mission language, target scope — will naturally evolve as your thinking deepens. That's expected.

**Q: What if my concept overlaps with an existing organization?**
A: That's fine, and frankly realistic. Multiple organizations often work on the same issue. What differentiates yours might be geography, population, approach, or a gap that existing organizations aren't addressing.

**Q: Can I use AI for this assignment?**
A: Absolutely — use whatever tools help you think. The final document should reflect your own thinking and voice. AI is a useful brainstorming partner, not a substitute for your judgment.

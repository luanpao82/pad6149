## What Do I Submit?

### 1. Organization Name — Verified and Final

You've been using a working name since Week 1. Before you go any further, you need to confirm that your name is **legally available and registrable**. A name collision with an existing entity can block your incorporation and trademark down the line.

**How to verify your name:**

| Check | Where | What You're Looking For |
|---|---|---|
| **Florida business name** | [Sunbiz.org](https://sunbiz.org) → Search by Name | No active corporation, LLC, or nonprofit with the same or confusingly similar name |
| **Federal trademark** | [USPTO TESS](https://tess2.uspto.gov) → Basic Word Mark Search | No registered or pending trademark in your service area |
| **Domain availability** | [Namecheap](https://www.namecheap.com) or any registrar | Check if a .org domain is available (you don't need to buy it yet) |
| **Social media handles** | Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn | Check if your preferred handle is available on major platforms |
| **IRS EIN database** | [IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search](https://apps.irs.gov/app/eos/) | No existing 501(c)(3) with the same name in your state |

If your name is taken, this is the week to adjust it — not after you've built 10 pages of content around it. Try **Namelix** (namelix.com) — an AI-powered name generator that creates name ideas with logo concepts based on your keywords.

> [!tip] AI Tip: Name Alternatives
> If your first choice is taken, try:
> ```
> My nonprofit's working name "[Your Name]" is already registered in
> Florida. The organization serves [population] in [area] and focuses
> on [issue]. Suggest 10 alternative names that are distinctive,
> mission-aligned, and unlikely to conflict with existing organizations.
> ```

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Name Verification
> - **Sunbiz.org search:** No active entity named "Asian Bridge Orlando" in Florida ✓
> - **USPTO TESS:** No registered trademark for "Asian Bridge" in nonprofit services ✓
> - **Domain:** asianbridgeorlando.org — available ✓
> - **Social:** @asianbridgeorl available on Instagram and Facebook ✓
> - **IRS EOS:** No existing 501(c)(3) with this name in Florida ✓
>
> Name confirmed. If "Asian Bridge Orlando" had been taken, alternatives considered were "Asia Connect Orlando" and "Orlando Asian Community Alliance."

Once verified, this becomes your **official name** going forward across all documents and your website.

### 2. Final Mission Statement

You've been working with a draft since Week 1. Now refine it. A strong nonprofit mission statement is:
- **Concise** — under 25 words if possible
- **Action-oriented** — starts with a verb or clearly states what you do
- **Specific** — names the population and the change you seek
- **Memorable** — someone should be able to repeat it after hearing it once

> [!tip] AI Tip: Mission Statement Refinement
> Upload your Concept Statement (Week 1), Board Governance Policy (Week 4), and HR Plan (Week 5) to NotebookLM. Then ask:
> ```
> Based on everything I've written about this organization so far,
> help me refine this mission statement. Current draft: "[your draft]."
> Make it under 25 words, action-oriented, and specific about who
> we serve and what change we create. Give me 5 options.
> ```
> Pick the one that resonates, or combine elements from several.

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Mission Refinement
> **Draft (Week 1):** "Asian Bridge Orlando provides culturally responsive social services, language access programs, and community navigation support for Asian American and immigrant families in Central Florida to reduce isolation and strengthen civic participation."
>
> **Final (Week 6):** "Asian Bridge Orlando connects Central Florida's Asian American and immigrant families to the services, language access, and community they need to thrive."
>
> Shorter, clearer, same substance. The full detail lives in the About page — the mission statement is the headline.

### 3. Vision Statement

Your mission says what you do. Your **vision** says what the world looks like when you've succeeded. The vision is aspirational — it describes the future you're working toward, not your current activities.

A strong vision statement is:
- **Future-oriented** — describes a changed reality, not your programs
- **Inspiring** — motivates staff, donors, and community members
- **Ambitious but credible** — aspirational without being vague or grandiose

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Vision Statement
> **Mission:** "Asian Bridge Orlando connects Central Florida's Asian American and immigrant families to the services, language access, and community they need to thrive."
>
> **Vision:** "A Central Florida where every immigrant family — regardless of language or background — can fully access the opportunities, services, and civic life of their community."
>
> The mission is about what we do today. The vision is about why it matters — and what success looks like at scale.

### 4. Tagline

A tagline is the short phrase that appears alongside your logo. It captures the spirit of the organization in a few words.

Examples from real nonprofits:
- Habitat for Humanity: *"Building strength, stability and self-reliance"*
- United Way: *"Live United"*
- Doctors Without Borders: *"Medical aid where it's needed most"*

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Tagline
> "Bridging Communities. Building Futures."

### 5. Logo (Final Version)

If you created a quick logo in Week 3, now is the time to refine it. Use Canva AI to create something professional.

Your logo should:
- Work at small sizes (social media profile) and large sizes (banner)
- Be readable in black and white
- Reflect your mission without being overly literal

For font pairing, **Fontjoy** (fontjoy.com) uses AI to suggest heading + body font combinations that match your brand's tone — enter one font you like and it generates complementary pairings.

> [!tip] AI Tip: Logo Refinement with Canva
> In Canva, try **Magic Design** or **Text to Image**:
> ```
> Create a minimalist logo for "Asian Bridge Orlando," a nonprofit
> connecting immigrant families to community resources. Style: modern,
> warm, professional. Incorporate a subtle bridge or connection motif.
> Colors: teal and gold.
> ```
> Download in PNG (transparent background) and SVG if available.

### 6. Brand Color Palette

Define 3–4 colors:
- **Primary** — your dominant brand color (headers, buttons, logo)
- **Secondary** — accent color (highlights, call-to-action elements)
- **Neutral** — background and body text colors

Include hex codes so your brand is consistent across the website, documents, and presentations. Use **Coolors.co** to generate harmonious palettes from your primary color, and run your text/background combinations through the **WebAIM Contrast Checker** (webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker) to verify they meet WCAG AA accessibility standards (minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio).

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Color Palette
> | Role | Color | Hex Code |
> |---|---|---|
> | Primary | Deep Teal | #0D7377 |
> | Secondary | Warm Gold | #E8A838 |
> | Background | Off-White | #FAFAF7 |
> | Text | Dark Charcoal | #1C1917 |

### 7. Target Audiences

Define 3–4 distinct audiences your organization communicates with. Each audience has different needs and receives different messages:

| Audience | What They Need to Know | How You Reach Them |
|---|---|---|
| **Beneficiaries / Clients** | What services you offer, how to access them, that you're trustworthy | Community events, word of mouth, multilingual materials, partner referrals |
| **Donors / Funders** | Your impact, your credibility, how their money makes a difference | Website, grant applications, annual reports, social media |
| **Volunteers** | What roles are available, what the experience is like, why it matters | Website "Get Involved" page, university partnerships, community boards |
| **Partners** | What you do, how collaboration benefits both parties, your track record | Direct outreach, conferences, shared networks |

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Audience Notes
> A key insight for Asian Bridge Orlando: the beneficiary audience is not monolithic. Communications for Korean-speaking families look different from communications for Burmese refugee families — not just in language, but in tone, channel, and cultural framing. The marketing strategy must account for this internal diversity rather than treating "Asian American" as a single audience.

### 8. You Are the Executive Director

This is your organization — so you are its founding **Executive Director**. Starting this week, put yourself on the website as the ED. This isn't hypothetical; you're building the case that you could actually lead this organization.

**What to prepare:**
- A **professional headshot** (or a clean, appropriate photo — phone camera is fine)
- A **brief professional bio** (150–200 words, third person) highlighting your education, relevant experience, and why you're the right person to lead this organization
- A **one-page resume or CV** tailored to this role — emphasize MNM coursework, relevant professional experience, and any sector-specific skills (grant writing, volunteer management, financial analysis, etc.)

Save the resume as PDF. You'll upload it to your website and link to it from your Team page.

> [!tip] AI Tip: Executive Director Bio
> ```
> Write a 150-word professional bio for [Your Name], founding Executive
> Director of [Your Org Name]. Background: MPA/MNM student at UCF,
> experience in [your relevant experience]. The organization serves
> [population]. Write in third person, professional tone. This will
> appear on the organization's website.
> ```
> Edit the output to make sure it sounds like you — not like a template.

> [!example] Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — ED Profile
> **Name:** [Student Name], M.N.M.
> **Title:** Founding Executive Director
>
> **Bio:** "[Student Name] is the founding Executive Director of Asian Bridge Orlando. She holds a Master of Nonprofit Management from the University of Central Florida, where her research focused on culturally responsive service delivery for immigrant communities. Prior to founding Asian Bridge Orlando, she served as a program coordinator at the Asian American Resource Center of Central Florida, where she managed community navigation services for over 200 families annually. A first-generation Korean American, she brings both professional expertise and lived experience to the organization's mission of connecting Central Florida's Asian American and immigrant families to essential services and community."
>

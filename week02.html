What Is This Assignment?

Last week you defined a nonprofit concept. This week you're identifying someone who's already doing the work — and preparing to learn from them.

The deliverable is straightforward: identify a nonprofit leader in your field, explain why you chose them, and draft the questions you'll ask in an informational interview. This sets up the Mentor Interview Report (due Week 4, 10 pts).

Think of this as the research and preparation phase. The stronger your plan, the better the interview. Also start thinking about logistics — if you plan to record the interview (always with permission), Otter.ai (otter.ai) provides free AI transcription that can save you hours of note-taking when writing the Week 4 report.

Why Does This Matter?

You've spent the MNM program studying nonprofit management in the classroom. This assignment moves you into the field. An informational interview with an experienced practitioner will give you insights that readings and case studies can't — how decisions actually get made, what keeps leaders up at night, and what they wish they'd known earlier.

For many of you already working in the sector, this is also a networking opportunity. The nonprofit world is small, and the connections you make in this assignment can extend well beyond this course.

What Do I Submit?

A 1–2 page document with three sections:

1. Who Will You Contact?

Identify your interview target:

  • Name (if identified — otherwise, describe your Plan B)
  • Title (Executive Director, Program Director, Founder, Board Chair, etc.)
  • Organization name
  • Connection to your concept — Why this person and this organization? How does their work relate to the nonprofit you're building?

Look for a role-model organization: one that operates in a similar space, serves a similar population, or uses an approach you want to learn from. They don't need to be local — a Zoom call works.

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Who to Contact

Name: Sarah Kim, Executive Director Organization: Asian American Resource Center of Central Florida Why: AARC is the primary organization serving Asian American communities in the Orlando area, offering translation services, immigration legal clinics, and cultural programs. Their work directly aligns with what Asian Bridge Orlando aims to provide. I want to understand how they built their model, what funding challenges they face, and where they see unmet needs — particularly for South Asian and Southeast Asian populations that may be underserved by existing programs.

2. Why This Organization?

Write 3–5 sentences connecting this organization to your nonprofit concept. What can you learn from them? What do they do well? What gaps might exist that your organization could address?

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Why This Org

The Asian American Resource Center (AARC) has served Central Florida's Asian community for over 15 years, with established programs in language access and immigration legal services. Their model is directly relevant to Asian Bridge Orlando's design. However, initial research suggests that AARC's programming focuses primarily on East Asian communities — Chinese, Korean, and Vietnamese populations. There may be a significant service gap for South Asian and Southeast Asian families, particularly Burmese, Nepali, and Bhutanese refugees who have been resettling in the Orlando area in growing numbers. Understanding AARC's experience and perspective on these gaps will be critical for defining Asian Bridge Orlando's niche.

3. Interview Questions (8–10)

Draft 8–10 substantive questions. The best questions ask about experience, judgment, and lessons learned — things you can't find on a website or Form 990.

Avoid: - Questions answerable via Google or GuideStar ("What is your mission?" "What's your annual budget?") - Yes/no questions that shut down conversation

Aim for: - Open-ended questions that draw out professional insight - Questions specific enough to show you've done your homework - At least 2–3 questions that connect directly to your own nonprofit concept

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Interview Questions

  1. How has AARC's mission evolved since its founding, and what drove those changes?
  2. What are the most significant unmet needs you see in Central Florida's Asian American and immigrant community today?
  3. How do you navigate the considerable diversity within the Asian American community — different languages, immigration histories, cultural norms — when designing programs?
  4. What does your funding model look like, and how has it shifted over the years?
  5. What has been your biggest challenge in building trust with the communities you serve?
  6. How do you recruit and retain bilingual, bicultural staff and volunteers? What's worked and what hasn't?
  7. What role does policy advocacy play in your work? How do you navigate 501(c)(3) limitations?
  8. If you were starting this organization today, what would you do differently?
  9. Are there populations or needs in the area that you feel are currently underserved — groups that aren't being reached by existing organizations?
  10. What advice would you give to someone launching a new nonprofit in this space?

Using AI to Draft Questions

AI can help generate a starting list, but edit aggressively — generic questions signal that you haven't done your homework.

I'm interviewing the Executive Director of a nonprofit serving Asian
American and immigrant families in Central Florida. My own nonprofit
will focus on [your specific angle]. Draft 10 interview questions
focused on lessons learned, organizational challenges, and strategic
decisions — not factual information I could find online.

Review the output critically. Cut anything generic, and add questions that reflect your specific research on this organization.

Getting the Interview: Outreach

A brief note on making contact, since this directly affects your Week 4 deliverable.

Outreach best practices: - Subject line should be clear and professional: "UCF Graduate Student — 30-Minute Informational Interview Request" - Keep the email concise — 5–6 sentences - Reference something specific about their work (shows you've done your research) - Offer flexibility on format and timing - Send it this week. The interview needs to happen before the Week 4 report is due.

Before you send, run your email through Grammarly (grammarly.com) for tone and clarity — first impressions matter, and a polished email signals professionalism. To simplify scheduling, consider including a Calendly (calendly.com) link so they can pick a time that works without back-and-forth emails.

Have a backup plan. Identify 2–3 potential contacts in case your first choice is unavailable.

Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Outreach Email

Subject: UCF Graduate Student — 30-Minute Interview Request

Dear Ms. Kim,

My name is [Student Name], and I'm completing my final semester in the Master of Nonprofit Management program at the University of Central Florida. As part of my capstone course, I'm developing a nonprofit concept focused on serving Asian American and immigrant families in Central Florida — particularly populations that may be underserved by existing community resources.

I've been following the work of the Asian American Resource Center, and your language access programs and community legal clinics are a model for the kind of organization I'm hoping to build. I would greatly appreciate the opportunity to learn from your experience — what has shaped AARC's approach, what challenges you've navigated, and where you see opportunities in this space.

Would you be available for a 30-minute phone or Zoom conversation in the next two weeks? I'm happy to work around your schedule.

Thank you for your time and your work in our community.

Sincerely, [Student Name] Master of Nonprofit Management, University of Central Florida

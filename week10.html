What Is This Assignment?

You have a strategic plan with goals and objectives. Now the question every funder, board member, and stakeholder will ask: how do you know it's working?

In PAD 6327, you developed a Program Evaluation Plan for an existing nonprofit — designing evaluation questions, selecting methods, identifying data sources, and proposing an analysis framework. Now you're designing evaluation from the ground up for your own organization, with the advantage of building measurement into your programs from day one instead of retrofitting it later.

This is a significant advantage. Most nonprofits struggle with evaluation because they design programs first and think about measurement after. You're doing it the right way — defining what success looks like before you launch.

What Do I Submit?

1. Program Descriptions (1–2 pages)

Before you can evaluate, you need to clearly define what you're evaluating. Describe your organization's 2–3 core programs or service areas. For each program, include:

  • Program name
  • Target population for this specific program
  • Activities and services provided
  • Expected outputs (what you produce — numbers of sessions, workshops, referrals)
  • Expected outcomes (what changes — knowledge gained, services accessed, barriers reduced)
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Program 1

Program: Community Navigation Services

Target population: First-generation Asian American and immigrant families with limited English proficiency in the Orlando metro area

Activities:

  • One-on-one navigation sessions with bilingual Community Navigators
  • Intake assessments in client's preferred language
  • Referrals to healthcare, legal, educational, and social service providers
  • Follow-up contacts to ensure successful service connection

Expected outputs (Year 1):

  • 150 families served through individual navigation
  • 600+ referrals made to partner agencies
  • 85% of clients contacted for at least one follow-up

Expected outcomes:

  • 70% of referred clients successfully connect with the service they were referred to
  • Clients report reduced barriers to service access (pre/post survey)
  • Clients report increased knowledge of available community resources

2. Logic Model (1 page)

Create a logic model for your primary program. A logic model maps the relationship between resources, activities, outputs, and outcomes — it's the visual blueprint for how your program creates change.

Use Canva to create a clean, visual logic model — or build it as a table in your document.

The logic model makes visible the theory of change: bilingual navigation → service connection → reduced barriers → improved life outcomes. Each link in this chain needs to be measured.

3. Evaluation Questions (half a page)

Define 3–5 evaluation questions that your framework is designed to answer. Good evaluation questions are:

  • Directly tied to your strategic goals (Week 9)
  • Answerable with data you can realistically collect
  • Useful for decision-making — the answers should tell you what to do next
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Evaluation Questions
  1. Are community navigation services reaching the populations most in need (particularly families with limited English proficiency)?
  2. Are referred clients successfully connecting with the services they were referred to, and if not, what barriers remain?
  3. Do clients report reduced barriers to service access and increased knowledge of community resources after receiving navigation services?
  4. Are community workshops increasing participants' ability to independently navigate systems (healthcare, education, civic participation)?
  5. Is our volunteer program effectively supporting service delivery without compromising quality?

4. Data Collection Plan (1–2 pages)

For each evaluation question, describe:

  • What data you'll collect (quantitative, qualitative, or both)
  • How you'll collect it (surveys, interviews, case file review, observation, administrative data)
  • When you'll collect it (intake, discharge, quarterly, annually)
  • Who is responsible for data collection

Be realistic about capacity. A 5-person startup cannot run a randomized controlled trial. Focus on methods that are practical, low-cost, and built into your existing service delivery workflow.

5. Analysis and Reporting Plan (half a page)

How will you make sense of the data and communicate findings?

  • How often will you analyze and review data? (quarterly is standard for a startup)
  • Who reviews the findings? (ED, board, program staff)
  • How will findings be shared? (board reports, annual report, funder reports, website updates)
  • How will evaluation results inform program improvements? (describe a feedback loop)
Running Example: Asian Bridge Orlando — Reporting Cycle
  • Monthly: Community Navigators log service data in Google Sheets; Program Director reviews for trends
  • Quarterly: Program Director compiles a 2-page dashboard for the board (# families served, referral success rate, client satisfaction scores)
  • Annually: ED produces a public impact report summarizing Year 1 outcomes — published on website and shared with all donors and funders
  • Continuous improvement: Quarterly staff meetings review evaluation data and identify program adjustments. If referral success rate drops below 60%, the team investigates barriers and modifies the process.

6. Ethical Considerations (half a page)

Address evaluation ethics specific to your population:

  • How will you protect client confidentiality, especially for vulnerable populations?
  • How will you obtain informed consent for data collection?
  • What cultural considerations apply to your evaluation methods? (e.g., survey language, interview norms, trust building)
  • How will you ensure evaluation doesn't create burden for the people you serve?

Use Perplexity.ai to research ethical evaluation practices for your specific population: “What are the ethical considerations for evaluating programs serving immigrant families with limited English proficiency? How should informed consent processes be adapted?”
